# Real material data

The constitutive models in [Part 2](../20-materials/00-trait-hierarchy.md) are useless without real numbers to calibrate them to. This chapter collects the material data — stress–strain curves, viscoelastic spectra, thermal coefficients, conductivity versus filler fraction — that underpins every concrete example in the book. The data is drawn from manufacturer technical data sheets, published characterization studies, and (where neither is available) our own planned measurement protocol (the viscoelastic side of which is the [Part 2 Ch 07 DMA protocol](../20-materials/07-viscoelastic.md)).

Three material families cover the design space the book cares about: platinum-cure silicone at the soft end (Ecoflex), platinum-cure silicone at the tough end (Dragon Skin), and carbon-black-loaded conductive silicone for electrical readout and Joule heating. Between them they span Shore 00-10 to Shore 30A in stiffness, from near-zero to moderate viscous dissipation, and from insulating to percolation-threshold conductivities. Every application the canonical problem (Ch 00) stands in for — seals, grippers, sheaths, wearables — uses one or more of these three.

| Material family | Stiffness range | Why this family |
|---|---|---|
| [Ecoflex family](04-material-data/00-ecoflex.md) | Shore 00-10 to 00-50, $E \approx 55$ kPa to ~220 kPa (small-strain) | Smooth-On Ecoflex 00-10/20/30/50 — the workhorse platinum-cure silicone of soft robotics; hyperelastic curve fits published in the silicone-characterization literature for 00-30 and 00-50 ([hyperelasticity reference leaf](../appendices/00-references/01-hyperelastic.md) — specific citations filled at Pass 3); elastomer of first resort when conformity dominates |
| [Dragon Skin family](04-material-data/01-dragonskin.md) | Shore 10A to 30A, $E \approx 300$ kPa to ~1.5 MPa | Smooth-On Dragon Skin 10A/20A/30A — higher Shore A, tougher, tear-resistant; used when Ecoflex fails under repeated loading or sharp-edged contact |
| [Carbon-black-loaded composites](04-material-data/02-carbon-black.md) | Varies with filler fraction (5–20 wt%) | Conductive silicone for capacitive/resistive sensing and Joule-heating — the bridge to [`sim-thermostat`](../20-materials/08-thermal-coupling.md) for temperature coupling and to electrical readout in Part 10's reward terms |

Two claims Ch 04 rests on:

1. **We use real measured data, not textbook placeholders.** Every stiffness, density, viscoelastic relaxation time, and thermal coefficient in the book is traceable to either a cited publication or a planned measurement. The material sub-chapters name the source on each value. This matters because the Ch 02 material-poverty failure is a failure of data as much as a failure of constitutive form — the shape of the Ogden curve is only as useful as the parameters fit to a real silicone.
2. **The families chosen span the canonical problem's range.** A compliant cavity optimized against a rigid probe needs a material soft enough to conform (Ecoflex), a material tough enough to survive repeated cycling (Dragon Skin), and at least one material that doubles as a sensor (carbon-black composite). The three families together are sufficient for the canonical problem and for the related problems in [Ch 00's related-problems section](00-canonical/01-related-problems.md); additional families (fluorosilicones, polyurethanes, hydrogels) are noted where relevant but not characterized in this chapter.
