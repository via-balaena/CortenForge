# Dragon Skin family

Dragon Skin is Smooth-On's platinum-cure silicone line in the Shore A durometer range, where [Ecoflex](00-ecoflex.md) leaves off. `sim-soft` uses Dragon Skin as the tough counterpart to Ecoflex in the canonical problem, per [Part 1 Ch 04 — material data](../04-material-data.md): same cure chemistry (platinum-catalyzed, room-temperature, Smooth-On's standard two-part liquid), same handling, but roughly 5× the small-strain modulus range and substantially higher tear resistance. It is the material called for when Ecoflex cannot survive the loading cycle — repeated contact against a sharp-edged partner, high-frequency actuation, or long-duration cyclic drive. The three sub-chapters split the family's data story; the [appendices material database](../../appendices/02-material-db.md) carries the per-grade nominal values this branch refers back to, so this page stays narrative rather than re-tabling numbers.

Four claims the Dragon Skin branch rests on:

1. **Chemistry continuity with Ecoflex is the architectural win.** Dragon Skin and Ecoflex are both platinum-cure silicones from the same manufacturer's compatible-line products, so a multi-material mesh (per [Part 2 Ch 09 — spatial fields](../../20-materials/09-spatial-fields.md)) can ship a stiff Dragon Skin 20A core bonded to a soft Ecoflex 00-30 shell without a chemistry interface: the cure chemistry is the same, the co-cure bond is robust, and the `MaterialField` spatial function just transitions $\mu(x)$ and the per-region parameters across the interface. A heterogeneous soft-robot design — stiff skeletal elements inside a soft skin — becomes one continuous molding process rather than a two-part assembly with an adhesive boundary.

2. **The grade ladder trades stretch for toughness.** Dragon Skin 10A reaches roughly 1000% elongation at break, 20A drops to around 620%, and 30A drops further to about 364%, per the [material database](../../appendices/02-material-db.md). Meanwhile small-strain modulus climbs from roughly 300 kPa to 1.5 MPa — a 5× range. The designer picks a grade against two concerns at once: how stiff does the part need to be, and how far does it need to stretch before tearing. For the canonical problem's cycling-under-squeeze regime, 10A and 20A are the working range; 30A enters when sharp-edged contact or stiffness is the dominant concern and maximum stretch is not.

3. **Published characterization is thinner than Ecoflex's, so per-print calibration carries more weight.** Dragon Skin is less commonly studied in the soft-robotics characterization literature than Ecoflex — most academic papers default to Ecoflex as the test material of choice. The [hyperelastic curve fits sub-chapter](01-dragonskin/01-hyperelastic-fits.md) names the few published fits, and the [material database](../../appendices/02-material-db.md) marks most Dragon Skin rows as Shore-A-correlation-derived with measurement-at-Pass-3 scheduled. Dragon Skin's characterization-coverage gap is why the [Part 10 Ch 05 sim-to-real loop](../../100-optimization/05-sim-to-real.md) is load-bearing for this family specifically: per-print calibration closes the data gap in the absence of a broad literature.

4. **The Mooney-Rivlin operating window is where most Dragon Skin runs live.** Where Ecoflex pushed to its elongation-at-break limit is where [Ogden](../../20-materials/04-hyperelastic/02-ogden.md) wins, Dragon Skin on the canonical problem sits mostly in the moderate-stretch regime — 50 to 150% — which is the [Mooney-Rivlin](../../20-materials/04-hyperelastic/01-mooney-rivlin.md) sweet spot called out in [Part 2 Ch 04's Mooney-Rivlin branch](../../20-materials/04-hyperelastic/01-mooney-rivlin.md) third claim. Dragon Skin 10A and 20A under cyclic loading hit this window cleanly; 30A under large squeeze can enter the regime where Ogden takes over, and the [hyperelastic-fits sub-chapter](01-dragonskin/01-hyperelastic-fits.md) therefore ships both Mooney-Rivlin and Ogden fits for that boundary case.
