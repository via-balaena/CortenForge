# Carbon-black-loaded composites

Carbon-black-loaded silicone is `sim-soft`'s multi-property material — a single filler-fraction parameter shifts the mechanical, electrical, and thermal response at once. The base elastomer is either [Ecoflex 00-30](00-ecoflex.md) or [Dragon Skin 10A](01-dragonskin.md), and the carbon-black loading runs from 5 to 20 wt% (per the [material database](../../appendices/02-material-db.md)). Per [Part 1 Ch 04 — material data](../04-material-data.md), this is the family that doubles as a sensor: conductive above percolation, capacitively coupled near it, and Joule-heatable once current can flow. The four sub-chapters split the family's property story into its axes; percolation gets its own sub-chapter because the electrical transition is structurally different from the gradual mechanical and thermal shifts that accompany it.

| Section | What it covers |
|---|---|
| [Concentration → modulus](02-carbon-black/00-modulus.md) | Small-strain modulus as a monotonic multiplier of the base silicone's $E$ (roughly 1.2× at 5 wt% up to ≈2.8× at 20 wt%); the mechanical implications for the stiff-skin-on-soft-core design pattern |
| [Concentration → electrical conductivity](02-carbon-black/01-conductivity.md) | Many-orders-of-magnitude jump across the percolation threshold; the parameter that drives sensing and Joule-heating readout in [Part 10 Ch 06 — full loop](../../100-optimization/06-full-loop.md) |
| [Concentration → thermal conductivity](02-carbon-black/02-thermal.md) | Modest monotonic increase with loading (roughly 1.1× to 2.0× the base); the parameter [`sim-thermostat`](../../20-materials/08-thermal-coupling.md) reads for heat transport and dissipative heating |
| [Percolation threshold](02-carbon-black/03-percolation.md) | The ≈8–10 wt% filler-fraction window where the composite transitions from insulator to resistive conductor; the structural reason electrical conductivity is not a smooth function of filler fraction |

Four claims the carbon-black branch rests on:

1. **One filler parameter moves three property axes simultaneously.** Dialing carbon-black loading is not a single-axis decision — the same filler fraction that raises the modulus by 1.2–2.8× also raises the thermal conductivity by 1.1–2.0× and shifts the electrical conductivity across orders of magnitude. [Part 2 Ch 09 — spatial fields](../../20-materials/09-spatial-fields.md)'s `MaterialField` framing is what lets `sim-soft` treat this honestly: a single carbon-black-fraction field over the body drives multiple derived parameters, rather than the mechanical, electrical, and thermal parameters being independent design variables the optimizer could in principle dial separately. They cannot, because physically they are not independent.

2. **Percolation is the central phenomenon, and it is structurally not a smooth curve.** Below the percolation threshold — roughly 8–10 wt% for these base silicones, per the [material database](../../appendices/02-material-db.md) — the composite is an insulator; the carbon-black particles do not form a conducting network. Above threshold, a percolating cluster spans the body and the composite becomes a resistive conductor. The transition is sharp: several orders of magnitude in conductivity across a few percent of filler fraction. The [percolation-threshold sub-chapter](02-carbon-black/03-percolation.md) walks the statistical-physics argument; the consequence for `sim-soft` is that electrical conductivity is not a smooth function of filler fraction, and any optimizer working on electrical reward terms must either stay on one side of the threshold or handle the discontinuity explicitly.

3. **Sensing and Joule heating are the applications the family is included for.** Ecoflex and Dragon Skin are mechanical materials; the carbon-black composite is the material that bridges to electrical. The canonical problem's sensing loop (capacitive or resistive readout of contact pressure at the compliant cavity surface) and the Joule-heating loop (current driven through a carbon-black skin to raise the local temperature field, which feeds back into the [`sim-thermostat`](../../20-materials/08-thermal-coupling.md) coupling) both live in this family exclusively. Without a carbon-black composite in the material palette, the [Part 10 Ch 06 full design-print-rate loop](../../100-optimization/06-full-loop.md) has no electrical path from the part to the reward.

4. **Per-print calibration is not optional for this family.** The [appendices material database](../../appendices/02-material-db.md) carbon-black table flags its values as nominal and notes that composite behaviour depends sensitively on dispersion quality, cure profile, and aging. Mixing non-uniformity alone can shift the effective percolation threshold by several percent filler fraction; cure-profile variations and aging after cure both move the conductivity band further. The [Part 10 Ch 05 sim-to-real loop](../../100-optimization/05-sim-to-real.md)'s per-print residual GPs carry proportionally more weight for carbon-black rows than for Ecoflex or Dragon Skin rows — this is not a family where data-sheet numbers alone close the simulation-versus-reality gap.
