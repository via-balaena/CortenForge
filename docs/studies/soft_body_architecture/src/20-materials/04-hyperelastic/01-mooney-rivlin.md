# Mooney-Rivlin

Mooney-Rivlin is `sim-soft`'s two-parameter hyperelastic law, the middle tier in the [Part 2 Ch 04 — hyperelastic family](../04-hyperelastic.md) selector table. Where [neo-Hookean](00-neo-hookean.md) depends on the first invariant $I_1$ only, Mooney-Rivlin adds a $C_{01}(I_2 - 3)$ term — a linear combination of the first two invariants of $C = F^T F$ (per [Part 2 Ch 01 — strain measures](../01-strain-measures.md)). The three sub-chapters below walk the canonical two-parameter form ([Mooney 1940](../../appendices/00-references/01-hyperelastic.md#mooney-1940); [Rivlin 1948](../../appendices/00-references/01-hyperelastic.md#rivlin-1948)), the calibration procedure against silicone data, and the criteria that decide when the extra parameter is worth the extra calibration burden.

Four claims the Mooney-Rivlin branch rests on:

1. **The extra term buys $I_2$ sensitivity the neo-Hookean form cannot represent.** Neo-Hookean is structurally an $I_1$-only function; the $I_2$ direction of the invariant space is inert. Under uniaxial stretch the two invariants are close enough that this is invisible, which is why the neo-Hookean fit tracks uniaxial data so well; under biaxial or equibiaxial stretch — which is what happens where a compliant cavity wraps a rounded probe, per [Part 1 Ch 02 — rim deformation](../../10-physical/02-what-goes-wrong/04-rim.md) — the $I_2$ contribution becomes the difference between a fit that tracks the measured curve and one that drifts. Mooney-Rivlin is the minimum-complexity law that resolves this.

2. **Calibration needs more than a uniaxial curve.** For a two-parameter invariant law, the uniaxial Piola stress is $P = 2(\lambda - \lambda^{-2})(C_{10} + C_{01} \lambda^{-1})$ where $\lambda$ is the principal stretch. Over any realistic stretch range, the curves $\{1,\, \lambda^{-1}\}$ are close to linearly dependent against the data, which means a uniaxial-only fit admits a one-parameter family of $(C_{10}, C_{01})$ pairs that all track the uniaxial curve but disagree substantially on biaxial predictions. The [calibration sub-chapter](01-mooney-rivlin/01-calibration.md) enforces a mixed protocol — uniaxial plus biaxial, or uniaxial plus planar-shear — before `sim-soft` accepts a two-parameter fit as calibrated.

3. **The regime where Mooney-Rivlin wins is narrow and specific.** Below about 50% stretch, neo-Hookean and Mooney-Rivlin are indistinguishable within measurement error; at high stretch, both saturate and [Ogden](02-ogden.md) is the right law. Mooney-Rivlin's operating window is the moderate-stretch regime that most of the [canonical problem](../../10-physical/00-canonical.md)'s contact zones actually sit in — stiff enough that the $I_1$-only fit begins to drift, not so stiff that the $N{=}3$ Ogden form is needed. Dragon Skin 10A and 20A under moderate cyclic loading sit cleanly in this window (see the [material database](../../appendices/02-material-db.md)); Ecoflex pushed to its elongation limit does not.

4. **The cost over neo-Hookean is the price of one extra invariant evaluation.** The $I_2$ term and its first and second derivatives against $F$ add a block of arithmetic comparable to the $I_1$ block already present. Sparse assembly shape is identical — still a 12×12 (Tet4) or 30×30 (Tet10) per-element block, per [Part 3 Ch 00 — element choice](../../30-discretization/00-element-choice.md) — so the Newton outer loop of [Part 5 Ch 00](../../50-time-integration/00-backward-euler.md) sees no structural change. The extra Gauss-point cost is the honest price of the extra fit dimension, and it is the reason [Ch 04's default-is-not-best claim](../04-hyperelastic.md) matters: switching Mooney-Rivlin on globally where it is not warranted burns hot-path flops without moving the reward.
