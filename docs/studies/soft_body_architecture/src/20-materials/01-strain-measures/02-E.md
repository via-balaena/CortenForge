# Green strain E

$\mathbf{E} = \tfrac{1}{2}(C - I)$ is the finite-strain generalization of the infinitesimal strain tensor $\varepsilon$. The [strain-measures parent](../01-strain-measures.md) named Saint Venant–Kirchhoff and parts of the biomechanics literature as $\mathbf{E}$-based; this leaf writes $\mathbf{E}$ down, relates it to the small-strain limit, and explains why `sim-soft`'s default hyperelastic family does not use it.

The Green strain tensor is bolded $\mathbf{E}$ throughout this leaf to disambiguate from Young's modulus $E$ — the convention [Ch 02 linear](../02-linear.md) locked and [the notation appendix](../../appendices/03-notation.md) applies whenever both symbols share a page.

## Definition

In terms of the [right Cauchy-Green tensor $C$](01-C.md):

$$ \mathbf{E} = \tfrac{1}{2}(C - I) $$

Equivalently, in terms of the deformation gradient $F$:

$$ \mathbf{E} = \tfrac{1}{2}(F^T F - I) $$

and in terms of the displacement gradient $\nabla u = F - I$ where $u = x - X$:

$$ \mathbf{E} = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\nabla u + (\nabla u)^T + (\nabla u)^T \nabla u\right) $$

The first two terms of the last form are the infinitesimal strain $\varepsilon$; the third is the nonlinear correction. $\mathbf{E}$ is symmetric, rotation-invariant (inheriting rotation-invariance from $C$), and vanishes in the reference configuration ($\mathbf{E} = 0$ when $F = I$).

## Relation to the infinitesimal strain

In the small-strain limit, the $(\nabla u)^T \nabla u$ term is second-order and linear elasticity drops it:

$$ \mathbf{E} \approx \varepsilon = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\nabla u + (\nabla u)^T\right) $$

This is the approximation [Ch 02 linear](../02-linear.md) makes structurally — its Assumption 1 of infinitesimal strain. At 10% strain, the relative gap $\|\mathbf{E} - \varepsilon\| / \|\mathbf{E}\|$ is a few percent; at 50% stretch, it is around 17% (matching the linear-over-softening figure in Ch 02); at 100% stretch, around 30%. The reduction from $\mathbf{E}$ to $\varepsilon$ is what transitions a finite-strain hyperelastic derivation to its infinitesimal-strain linear counterpart.

## Saint Venant–Kirchhoff and why `sim-soft` does not default to it

Saint Venant–Kirchhoff is the simplest $\mathbf{E}$-based hyperelastic law:

$$ \psi_\text{SVK} = \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\, (\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{E})^2 + \mu\, \mathbf{E} : \mathbf{E} $$

It is Hooke's law written in $\mathbf{E}$ instead of $\varepsilon$ — rotation-invariant (because $\mathbf{E}$ is), closed-form tangent, and a natural first generalization beyond linear. `sim-soft` does not ship it as a first-class `Material` impl for two reasons.

**Bounded energy as $J \to 0$.** Under hydrostatic compression $F = J^{1/3} I$, SVK's energy evaluates to $\psi(J) = K(J^{2/3} - 1)^2$ with $K = (9\lambda + 6\mu)/8$. As $J \to 0^+$, $\psi(J)$ tends to the finite value $K$ rather than diverging — an SVK-modeled element can collapse toward zero volume with finite energy cost. Neo-Hookean's $(\ln J)^2$ volumetric term is unbounded as $J \to 0^+$, which is what makes it safe for the IPC-guarded deformations of the canonical problem: any configuration with an element approaching collapse pays unbounded energy and is repelled from the basin's boundary.

**Not in the silicone-fit family.** The [hyperelastic family in Ch 04](../04-hyperelastic.md) — neo-Hookean, Mooney-Rivlin, Ogden — is written against the invariants $I_1, I_2, I_3$ of $C$ and calibrated to silicone manufacturer data. SVK has no fit in this family; it is Hooke-on-$\mathbf{E}$, not a silicone-realistic curve. Adding it would not reduce fit error against any [Part 1 Ch 04 material](../../10-physical/04-material-data.md), and would introduce the compressive non-convexity as a failure mode the hyperelastic family does not carry.

## Where `sim-soft` still uses $\mathbf{E}$

$\mathbf{E}$ appears as a reporting and diagnostic field, not as a constitutive argument.

- **Strain-magnitude runtime warning.** The [validity-regime runtime warning](../00-trait-hierarchy/02-validity.md) uses $\|\mathbf{E}\|$ (Frobenius norm) as a scalar strain measure to compare against the impl's declared stretch bound. This converts the tensor to a single comparison scalar without depending on a constitutive law.
- **Regression tests against the linear small-strain reference.** The [gradcheck suite](../../110-crate/04-testing/03-gradcheck.md) compares linear-elastic predictions to neo-Hookean ones at matched strain states specified in $\mathbf{E}$; reporting in $\mathbf{E}$ makes the "six-digit match at 0.1% strain" assertion specific to the reference strain, not to the reference $F$.
- **Biomechanics-literature comparison.** When `sim-soft` compares against published biomech results (muscle, skin, vessel), the reference numbers are often reported in $\mathbf{E}$ or derived quantities. The [regression suite](../../110-crate/04-testing/01-regression.md) accepts $\mathbf{E}$-form inputs for cross-code comparison and converts to the internal $F$-form for solving.

## What this sub-leaf commits the book to

- **`sim-soft`'s hyperelastic default family is invariant-based, not $\mathbf{E}$-based.** Saint Venant–Kirchhoff is not in the default list; its compressive non-convexity is the specific reason. A user who wants SVK behavior outside the default list writes their own `impl Material`, declares a validity domain that excludes the non-convex regime, and the [gradcheck suite](../../110-crate/04-testing/03-gradcheck.md) enforces it.
- **$\mathbf{E}$ is a reporting measure, not a constitutive argument.** Where `sim-soft` exposes strain to users — runtime warnings, regression tests, cross-code comparisons — it uses $\mathbf{E}$ because it is the conventional finite-strain measure in the literature. Internal constitutive evaluation goes through $F$ and its invariants.
- **The bold-$\mathbf{E}$ convention holds wherever Young's modulus $E$ and Green strain share a page.** [Ch 02 linear](../02-linear.md) and [the notation appendix](../../appendices/03-notation.md) anchor this; every downstream chapter that uses both follows it.
