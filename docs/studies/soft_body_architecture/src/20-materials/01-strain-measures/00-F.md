# Deformation gradient F

The [strain-measures parent](../01-strain-measures.md) committed to $F$ as Part 2's primary kinematic. This leaf writes down what $F$ is, names the structural properties every `Material` implementation ([Ch 00 — trait surface](../00-trait-hierarchy/00-trait-surface.md)) relies on, and states how `sim-soft` computes it at each Gauss point.

## Definition

For a continuum mapping reference position $X$ to deformed position $x$, the deformation gradient is:

$$ F_{ij} = \frac{\partial x_i}{\partial X_j} $$

or in component-free notation $F = \partial x / \partial X$. Equivalently $F = I + \nabla_X u$ where $u = x - X$ is the displacement field. For a rigid translation $x = X + c$, $F = I$ (no deformation). For a pure rotation $x = R\, X$ with $R \in SO(3)$, $F = R$ (deformation from rigid rotation alone). For a pure stretch along a principal axis, $F$ is diagonal with the stretch ratios on the diagonal.

## Structural properties

Three properties every `sim-soft` solve enforces, and every constitutive law assumes.

**Orientation-preserving: $\det F > 0$.** A physical deformation cannot flip an element inside-out. Element inversion ($\det F \le 0$) is the inversion case on [`ValidityDomain::inversion`](../00-trait-hierarchy/02-validity.md) — constitutive laws declare how they handle it, and most diverge there. The [IPC barrier from Part 4](../../40-contact/01-ipc-internals/00-barrier.md) is what keeps $\det F$ bounded away from zero over a canonical-problem solve.

**Volume ratio $J = \det F$.** The scalar $J = \det F$ is the ratio of deformed to reference volume at the material point. For near-incompressible silicone, $J$ is driven toward 1 along the constraint; this is the quantity [Ch 05's incompressibility treatments](../05-incompressibility.md) project onto. The [notation appendix](../../appendices/03-notation.md) defines $J$ here.

**Polar decomposition $F = R\, U = V\, R$.** $F$ uniquely factors into a rotation $R \in SO(3)$ and a symmetric positive-definite stretch — right stretch $U$ and left stretch $V$, with $V = R\, U\, R^T$. This decomposition is what [Ch 03 corotational](../03-corotational.md) extracts per-element to isolate the rotation and apply Hooke's law to $U - I$; hyperelastic laws skip the decomposition because their energies are rotation-invariant by construction against $F$.

## Why `Material` takes $F$, not a derived strain measure

`sim-soft`'s [`Material` trait](../00-trait-hierarchy/00-trait-surface.md) signs its three methods in terms of $F$ — `energy(F) → ψ`, `first_piola(F) → P`, `tangent(F) → ∂P/∂F`. Four reasons.

**$F$ is the primary kinematic; every other measure is derived.** $C = F^T F$, $\mathbf{E} = \tfrac{1}{2}(C - I)$, invariants $I_1, I_2, I_3$, principal stretches $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3$ — all are closed-form functions of $F$. Writing the trait in terms of $F$ means each `Material` impl does its own derivation once rather than having the trait pre-compute a measure the impl doesn't use.

**First Piola $P = \partial \psi / \partial F$ is the natural stress for assembly.** The [FEM assembly VJP](../../60-differentiability/01-custom-vjps/01-fem-assembly.md) writes the per-element residual as $P$ contracted with the shape-function gradient $B$; it does not need Cauchy or second-Piola intermediates. Writing the trait in $F$ and $P$ minimizes conversion overhead along the assembly hot path.

**Autograd rules are simplest against $F$.** The [Part 6 Ch 01 VJP registration](../../60-differentiability/01-custom-vjps/00-registration.md) registers one VJP per `Material` impl — the pullback of $P$ through $\psi(F)$. Registering VJPs against $F$ rather than against derived measures avoids a stack of chain-rule conversions at differentiation time.

**Per-element shape knowledge stays in `element/`.** The [`element/`](../../110-crate/00-module-layout/01-element.md)'s shape-function gradient $B$ takes vertex positions to $F$ at each Gauss point; passing $F$ through the trait keeps shape-function concerns on the element side and constitutive concerns on the material side.

## Computing $F$ in `sim-soft`

Per-element, per-Gauss-point, via the shape-function gradient. For a Tet4 element with reference vertex positions $X_1, \ldots, X_4$ and deformed positions $x_1, \ldots, x_4$:

$$ F = \sum_{a=1}^{4} x_a \otimes \nabla_X N_a $$

where $N_a$ is the linear shape function for node $a$. This is one `Matrix3<f64>` per Gauss point, stored on the `element/` side and passed to the `Material` trait by reference. For Tet10 ([Ch 04 neo-Hookean](../04-hyperelastic/00-neo-hookean.md) and Phase H of [Part 11 Ch 03](../../110-crate/03-build-order.md)), the same formula runs over 10 nodes and 4 Gauss points per tet instead of 4 and 1.

## What $F$ does not carry

$F$ is purely kinematic. It carries the rigid rotation ($F = R$ under a pure rotation, with no strain implied); it does not know about stress, material parameters, or history. A pure rotation has $F \neq I$ but zero strain in every physically meaningful measure — $C = I$, $\mathbf{E} = 0$, $I_1 - 3 = 0$. A constitutive law that predicts nonzero stress under pure rotation ([Ch 02 linear](../02-linear.md)) is a law that reads the rotation-bearing part of $F$ as if it were strain.

## What this sub-leaf commits the book to

- **$F$ is the sole kinematic argument on the `Material` trait.** No alternate-measure overloads (`energy_from_C`, `first_piola_from_E`); every impl derives its needed measure from $F$. This keeps the trait surface at four items and every `Material` VJP at one registration.
- **$\det F > 0$ is a hard invariant of every solve.** The IPC barrier enforces it at the contact side; `sim-soft` does not attempt to continue a solve through an inverted element except via explicit opt-in on the `InversionHandling` slot of the impl's validity descriptor.
- **$J = \det F$ is the volumetric kinematic used by Ch 05 incompressibility and by downstream visualization.** Downstream Parts refer to $J$ without re-deriving it; the notation appendix defines it once.
