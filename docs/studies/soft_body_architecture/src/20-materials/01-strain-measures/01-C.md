# Right Cauchy-Green C

$C = F^T F$ is the symmetric, positive-definite, rotation-invariant form of the deformation gradient that invariant-based constitutive laws prefer over $F$ itself. The [strain-measures parent](../01-strain-measures.md) named Mooney-Rivlin, Ogden, and HGO as the invariant-based family; this leaf derives $C$ from [$F$](00-F.md), names its properties, and states where it appears in `sim-soft`'s hot path.

## Definition

For the deformation gradient $F$, the right Cauchy-Green deformation tensor is:

$$ C = F^T F $$

$C$ is a symmetric $3\times 3$ tensor with $C_{ij} = \sum_k F_{ki}\, F_{kj}$. The "right" label distinguishes it from the left Cauchy-Green $b = F F^T$; $C$ operates in reference coordinates (takes a reference-frame vector and measures its squared length in the deformed frame), $b$ operates in deformed coordinates. `sim-soft` works in reference coordinates throughout — reference mesh, reference-frame shape-function gradients, first-Piola stress — so $C$ is the form that appears on the constitutive side.

## Structural properties

Three properties every invariant-based constitutive law depends on, and every `sim-soft` implementation inherits.

**Symmetric.** $C^T = (F^T F)^T = F^T F = C$. A function $\psi(C)$ defined on symmetric matrices is automatically defined on $F^T F$ for every $F$; writing hyperelastic energies as $\psi(C)$ rather than $\psi(F)$ cuts the argument space from nine components to six.

**Positive-definite for $\det F > 0$.** For any nonzero vector $v$, $v^T C\, v = v^T F^T F\, v = \|F v\|^2 > 0$ whenever $F$ is invertible. This is what guarantees $C$'s eigenvalues — the squared principal stretches $\lambda_1^2, \lambda_2^2, \lambda_3^2$ — are strictly positive; a constitutive law written on $C$ can safely take square roots, logs, and fractional powers of these eigenvalues without branching on sign.

**Rotation-invariant.** Under $F \mapsto R F$ for any $R \in SO(3)$, $C = F^T F \mapsto F^T R^T R F = F^T F = C$ (using $R^T R = I$). Any function of $C$ is therefore automatically rotation-invariant in $F$ — the fictitious-rotation-stress failure from [Ch 02 linear](../02-linear.md) cannot occur for a law written on $C$. This is the structural reason [invariant-based hyperelastic laws](../04-hyperelastic.md) are rotation-invariant by construction rather than by after-the-fact extraction.

## Principal-stretch representation

$C$ is diagonalized by the right stretch $U$ from the [polar decomposition](00-F.md) $F = R U$: $C = U^2$, and $C$'s eigenvalues are the squared principal stretches $\lambda_1^2, \lambda_2^2, \lambda_3^2$. The [invariants sibling](03-invariants.md) writes $I_1, I_2, I_3$ in terms of these eigenvalues; the [Ogden branch](../04-hyperelastic/02-ogden.md) writes its energy directly in terms of the unsquared $\lambda_i$ via an eigen-decomposition of $C$ per Gauss point.

Computing the principal stretches requires one symmetric $3\times 3$ eigen-decomposition per Gauss point (closed-form via the characteristic polynomial or Jacobi iteration). At canonical-problem mesh sizes the aggregate eigen-decomposition cost is a small fraction of the per-Newton-step runtime, which is dominated by sparse assembly and factorization. The [Ogden stability sub-leaf](../04-hyperelastic/02-ogden/02-stability.md) handles the degenerate near-repeated-eigenvalue case where $\partial \lambda_i / \partial C$ would be ill-conditioned.

## Where `sim-soft` uses $C$

- **Mooney-Rivlin and Ogden energies** are written as $\psi(I_1, I_2)$ and $\psi(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3)$ respectively; both go through $C$ at Gauss-point-level evaluation ([Ch 04 MR](../04-hyperelastic/01-mooney-rivlin.md), [Ch 04 Ogden](../04-hyperelastic/02-ogden.md)).
- **Anisotropic $I_4 = a \cdot C\, a$** is defined on $C$, not $F$; see [Ch 06 fiber-reinforced](../06-anisotropic/00-fiber-reinforced.md) and the [invariants sibling](03-invariants.md).
- **Near-incompressibility mixed u-p** uses the isochoric split $\bar C = J^{-2/3} C$ to separate deviatoric response from volumetric constraint ([Ch 05 mixed u-p](../05-incompressibility/01-mixed-up.md)).

Neo-Hookean's canonical energy $\psi = \tfrac{\mu}{2}(I_1 - 3) - \mu\, \ln J + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}(\ln J)^2$ is also invariant-form and depends on $C$ through $I_1$; `sim-soft`'s NH impl evaluates $I_1 = \mathrm{tr}\,(F^T F)$ and $J = \det F$ directly from $F$ without materializing the full $C$ tensor (saves a few multiplies per Gauss point), but the conceptual dependency is on $C$'s invariants.

## What this sub-leaf commits the book to

- **Invariant-based constitutive laws work against $C$, not $F$ directly.** The base `Material` trait takes $F$ by signature ([Ch 00](../00-trait-hierarchy/00-trait-surface.md)), but each invariant-based impl derives $C$ from it and works with $C$'s invariants or eigenvalues. This derivation is what delivers rotation invariance structurally.
- **$C$ is not materialized when its invariants can be computed directly from $F$.** Neo-Hookean's evaluation path computes $I_1 = \mathrm{tr}\,(F^T F)$ and $J = \det F$ without forming the full $C$ matrix. The savings are small per Gauss point but multiply across canonical-problem tet counts.
- **Principal-stretch eigen-decomposition is a per-Gauss-point cost paid by Ogden and by mixed u-p.** Mooney-Rivlin avoids it by staying in $(I_1, I_2)$. The `Material` trait is uniform over both paths; the eigen-decomposition is an implementation detail of the Ogden and mixed-u-p impls.
