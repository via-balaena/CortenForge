# Thin-material stability

The previous two sub-leaves handled inter-layer interface types and multi-layer non-penetration at the solver level. This sub-leaf names the numerical regime where `sim-soft` most often encounters "IPC doesn't converge" reports — the thin-material regime, where a layer's thickness is comparable to the barrier activation distance $\hat d$ and the barriers on the layer's two surfaces overlap in their active bands.

## The barrier-overlap pathology

Consider a single thin layer of thickness $\ell$ sandwiched between two stiffer substrates. The top surface of the layer approaches substrate A at gap $d_A$; the bottom surface approaches substrate B at gap $d_B$. Both are inter-mesh contact pairs detected by the [Ch 03 broadphase](../03-self-contact/00-bvh.md); both contribute barrier energies $\kappa\, b(d_A, \hat d)$ and $\kappa\, b(d_B, \hat d)$ to the total potential.

The barrier is active whenever $d < \hat d$. If the layer is so thin that both gaps can be simultaneously below $\hat d$ — specifically when $\ell \lesssim 2\hat d$, since the two active bands each extend $\hat d$ inward from their respective surface — then **both barriers are active simultaneously** at the thin layer's interior vertices. Their gradients oppose: the barrier-A gradient pushes the layer away from A; the barrier-B gradient pushes it away from B. The gradients sum to a well-defined restoring force with a unique equilibrium (midpoint of the gap in the symmetric case), so the issue is not multiple equilibria — the barrier energies are each convex, and their sum is convex.

The pathology is **extreme ill-conditioning** near that equilibrium. Both barriers' second derivatives $b''(d)$ grow sharply as $d \to 0$ (the divergent part of the $(d-\hat d)^2 \ln(d/\hat d)$ form), and when $\ell \lesssim 2\hat d$ both surfaces' operating gaps are small absolute distances. The two $b''$ contributions add, giving the layer's interior vertex a very large tangent-direction stiffness — typically orders of magnitude larger than the elastic bulk stiffness the same vertex feels from its neighboring tets. The total Hessian's condition number spikes, the inner CG linear solve takes many more iterations to converge, and Newton's local quadratic model becomes a poor approximation over the step scale (the Hessian varies rapidly with small position changes, since $b''$'s gap-dependence is sharp in this regime). The Newton step routinely overshoots, the line search backtracks aggressively, and the adaptive-$\kappa$ schedule ([Ch 01 adaptive-kappa](../01-ipc-internals/01-adaptive-kappa.md)) can fire repeatedly as each trial step crosses through the narrow active-band region.

## The non-overlap condition

The barrier's active band on each surface extends a distance $\hat d$ inward from that surface. For a layer of thickness $\ell$, the two bands do not overlap when:

$$ \hat d < \ell / 2 $$

This is a dimensionless condition: $\hat d$ scales with $\ell$. For a 5 mm layer, $\hat d$ can be up to ~2.5 mm; for a 0.5 mm silicone film, $\hat d$ must stay below ~0.25 mm. The thickness scale is scene-dependent — what matters is not an absolute-millimeter threshold but the ratio $\hat d / \ell$ for each layer in the configuration.

The $\hat d < \ell/2$ rule is a practitioner-level engineering heuristic rather than a published theorem. [Li et al. 2020](../../appendices/00-references/00-ipc.md#li-2020)'s formulation treats $\hat d$ as a global scalar chosen from the scene's overall length scale and does not separately analyze the thin-layer regime. Li et al. 2021's C-IPC (codimensional IPC) extends IPC to codim-1 shells and codim-2 rods with prescribed thickness, but that paper's thickness mechanism is for surface and line primitives — not the codim-0 thin volumetric layer case this sub-chapter addresses. The $\hat d < \ell/2$ rule is the `sim-soft`-native distillation of the barrier-overlap pathology above; it applies to Ch 04's codim-0 thin-volumetric-layer regime.

## The small-$\hat d$ cost

Shrinking $\hat d$ to satisfy $\hat d < \ell_\text{min}/2$ at the thinnest layer is correctness-safe but costs Newton iterations:

- **Typical active-pair gap shrinks with $\hat d$.** After the [adaptive-$\kappa$ schedule](../01-ipc-internals/01-adaptive-kappa.md) equilibrates, operating gaps $d_k$ sit at a fraction of $\hat d$; shrinking $\hat d$ compresses the absolute gap range the barrier resolves, making small numerical errors in gap computation a larger fraction of the operating range.
- **Barrier Hessian conditioning degrades.** At fixed applied load, the equilibrium gap $d^\ast$ where barrier force balances load scales with $\hat d$ (the barrier's force profile over $(0, \hat d)$ rescales when $\hat d$ shrinks, pulling the equilibrium gap proportionally smaller). Since $b''(d)$ diverges as $d \to 0$, smaller $d^\ast$ means larger $b''$ at equilibrium, which raises the total Hessian's condition number and slows the inner CG linear solve.
- **Adaptive $\kappa$ has to work harder.** The growth rule from [Ch 01 adaptive-kappa](../01-ipc-internals/01-adaptive-kappa.md) triggers on observed gap closure; at smaller $\hat d$, gap closure happens over absolute-smaller distances, and more Newton iterations are spent triggering and responding to the growth rule before the barrier stiffens enough to hold the gap.

The operational trade-off: thin-material scenes typically need 2–5× more Newton iterations per timestep than same-resolution scenes with uniform-thickness layers. The [Ch 05 GPU lever](../05-real-time/00-gpu.md) amortizes some of this per-iterate cost, but the iteration-count multiplier is a structural overhead the thin-material regime carries even at fixed per-iteration cost.

## Per-pair adaptive $\hat d$

The resolution is **per-pair adaptive $\hat d$** rather than a single global $\hat d$ shrunken to the thinnest-layer worst case. Pairs at thick-layer interfaces use a larger $\hat d_k$ (cheap, well-conditioned barrier); pairs at thin-layer interfaces use a smaller $\hat d_k$ (expensive but stable). The global-worst-case alternative penalizes every pair in the scene with the thinnest-layer cost; per-pair adaptive $\hat d$ pays that cost only where needed.

The [IPC Toolkit](../../appendices/00-references/00-ipc.md#ipc-toolkit) reference implementation exposes per-primitive adaptive $\hat d$ through its SmoothCollisions variant: `vert_adaptive_dhat`, `edge_adaptive_dhat`, and `face_adaptive_dhat` are per-primitive `Eigen::VectorXd` arrays in `src/ipc/smooth_contact/smooth_collisions.hpp`, computed as $\hat d_k = \text{ratio} \cdot \sqrt{d_\text{contact}}$ with a user-configurable ratio. The standard (non-smooth) variant uses a global scalar $\hat d$ passed as a call-time argument to the barrier function (`barrier.hpp` line 19: `virtual double operator()(const double d, const double dhat) const`); the smooth variant's per-primitive adaptive $\hat d$ is the feature `sim-soft` ports for its thin-material support.

The specific `ratio · sqrt(contact_distance)` schedule produces a larger $\hat d_k$ for pairs with larger current gaps and a smaller $\hat d_k$ for pairs with already-small gaps. This matches the thin-material case: at a thin-layer interface where inter-mesh gaps are already small (inter-mesh gaps on the order of 0.1–0.5 mm for sub-millimeter layers in contact), the adaptive rule shrinks $\hat d_k$ proportionally, keeping the pair's $\hat d_k$ comfortably below the local thickness. [Ch 05 sub-chapter 01](../05-real-time/01-barrier-width.md) writes down the adaptive-$\hat d$ schedule in full; this sub-leaf's use of it is limited to establishing that per-pair $\hat d$ is the degree of freedom the thin-material regime exploits.

## Why thin-material is where convergence complaints cluster

`sim-soft`'s canonical-problem workload includes the [multi-layer cavity](../../10-physical/00-canonical/00-formulation.md) where a soft silicone liner of thickness $\sim 0.5$ mm sits inside a sleeve of thickness $\sim 2$ mm over a probe of thickness $\gg 10$ mm. The 0.5 mm liner is the thin-material case; a global $\hat d$ of even 0.25 mm gives $\hat d / \ell = 0.5$ — right at the boundary of the barrier-overlap regime — and Newton convergence degrades noticeably.

The "IPC doesn't converge" community reports (linked from [Ch 05 real-time](../05-real-time.md)) typically isolate to one of three causes: timestep too large (step larger than the characteristic time of the stiffest element); mesh too coarse near contact (barrier active-band spans multiple elements' worth of displacement); or — most commonly in multi-layer configurations — the thin-material regime where $\hat d$ is miscalibrated for the minimum layer thickness. This sub-leaf is the architectural answer to the third case; the others are addressed by [Part 5 Ch 00 backward-Euler](../../50-time-integration/00-backward-euler.md) and [Part 3 Ch 02 adaptive mesh refinement](../../30-discretization/02-adaptive.md) respectively.

## What this sub-leaf commits the book to

- **$\hat d$ is a per-pair parameter, not a scene-global scalar.** `sim-soft`'s `SoftScene` interface-pair metadata carries a per-pair $\hat d_k$ override alongside per-pair $\mu_c$ ([sub-chapter 00](00-sliding.md)). The global default is a fallback for uniform-thickness configurations; heterogeneous-thickness multi-layer configurations set per-pair $\hat d_k$ via the [Part 7 Ch 02 material-assignment API](../../70-sdf-pipeline/02-material-assignment.md) feeding the adaptive-$\hat d$ schedule from [Ch 05 sub-chapter 01](../05-real-time/01-barrier-width.md).
- **$\hat d < \ell/2$ is the correctness rule; per-pair adaptive $\hat d$ is the mechanism.** `sim-soft` does not expose a "thin-material mode" toggle. The adaptive $\hat d$ rule, with the ratio parameter tuned to the scene's thickness scale, produces $\hat d_k$ values satisfying $\hat d_k < \ell_k/2$ at thin-layer interfaces; the ratio-tuning is part of scene setup and does not introduce a new solver path.
- **The $\hat d < \ell/2$ rule is `sim-soft`-native practitioner heuristic.** [Li 2020](../../appendices/00-references/00-ipc.md#li-2020) treats $\hat d$ as a global scalar; C-IPC's thickness-enforcement is for codim-1 shells and codim-2 rods with prescribed thickness, not codim-0 thin volumetric layers. The codim-0 barrier-overlap failure mode is named here because the canonical-problem workload encounters it; the named fix is [IPC Toolkit](../../appendices/00-references/00-ipc.md#ipc-toolkit)'s per-primitive adaptive $\hat d$ from SmoothCollisions, ported through [Ch 05 sub-chapter 01](../05-real-time/01-barrier-width.md).
- **Thin-material is a cost regime, not a correctness regime.** With correct per-pair $\hat d$, the Ch 00 guarantees still hold at any layer thickness. The 2–5× Newton iteration count multiplier is a cost budget the [Ch 05 GPU lever](../05-real-time/00-gpu.md) amortizes across many pairs, not a correctness degradation.
