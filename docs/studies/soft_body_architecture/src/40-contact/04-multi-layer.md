# Multi-layer contact

Realistic soft parts are often layered — a stacked pair of sleeves sliding on a lubricated interface, a biological analog with skin over subcutaneous tissue over muscle, a compliant liner pressed between a stiff sleeve and a probe. This chapter covers inter-mesh contact between separately meshed layers ([Part 2 Ch 09](../20-materials/09-spatial-fields.md) covers multi-material fields within one mesh; [Part 3 Ch 03 bonded](../30-discretization/03-interfaces/00-bonded.md) covers single-mesh bonded multi-material configurations). IPC handles the contact at every inter-layer interface, but the real engineering question is stability: can the solver converge when the layers are much thinner than the canonical contact tolerance, and what happens when ten of them stack?

Two claims Ch 04 rests on:

1. **Multi-layer is the scaling case for IPC.** A single sleeve-on-probe problem has two surfaces and one inter-mesh contact interface; a three-layer biological analog has six surfaces and three inter-mesh interfaces; a ten-layer composite has twenty surfaces and nine interfaces, with per-primitive pair counts scaling accordingly. The per-pair cost of the IPC barrier has to be low, and the BVH acceleration from [Ch 03](03-self-contact.md) has to extend across meshes — the same BVH structure supports inter-mesh pair generation as well as intra-mesh self-contact.
2. **Thin materials are the stability case.** A 0.5 mm silicone film between a 5 mm substrate and a 20 mm probe has element aspect ratios that strain the solver, and the IPC barrier width $\hat d$ must satisfy $\hat d < \ell / 2$ for the minimum deformed layer thickness $\ell$ — otherwise the barrier's active bands from the layer's two surfaces overlap inside the layer, producing a non-convex energy landscape the Newton solver cannot handle. When $\hat d$ is too small (global-worst-case choice), the solver's adaptive stiffness schedule ([Ch 01 sub-chapter 01](01-ipc-internals/01-adaptive-kappa.md)) has to work harder at every pair, not just the thin ones. Per-pair adaptive $\hat d$ from [Ch 05 sub-chapter 01](05-real-time/01-barrier-width.md) resolves the trade-off by making $\hat d_k$ local to each pair. The thin-layer regime is where most "IPC doesn't converge" reports come from; [Ch 05](05-real-time.md) names this as a primary engineering lever.
