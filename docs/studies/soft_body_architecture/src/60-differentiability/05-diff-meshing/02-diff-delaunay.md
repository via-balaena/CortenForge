# Differentiable Delaunay

The marching-cubes-derived research lines [§01](01-diff-mc.md) surveyed attack the grid-based topology-classification step directly. A parallel line attacks the meshing problem through Delaunay triangulation instead — either by relaxing the connectivity choice into a continuous parameter (**[Rakotosaona et al. 2021](../../appendices/00-references/03-diff-sim.md#rakotosaona-2021)**) or by representing faces probabilistically over a weighted Delaunay tessellation (**[Son et al. 2024 (DMesh)](../../appendices/00-references/03-diff-sim.md#son-2024)**). The third and distinct approach — **[DMTet](../../appendices/00-references/03-diff-sim.md#shen-2021)** — keeps connectivity discrete but operates on a deforming background tet grid where topology changes happen rarely. This sub-leaf covers all three lines, names what each buys and costs, and explains why the DMTet family is the only one that has been deployed in production pipelines (NVIDIA [GET3D](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.11163), [Magic3D](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.10440)) while remaining outside `sim-soft`'s FEM reach.

## Rakotosaona 2021 — soft-relaxed weighted Delaunay

Rakotosaona, Aigerman, Mitra, Ovsjanikov & Guerrero 2021 (*Differentiable Surface Triangulation*, SIGGRAPH Asia 2021) formulates the connectivity of a weighted Delaunay triangulation as a *soft relaxation* of the hard empty-circumsphere predicate. The tessellation is parameterized by per-vertex weights; a face is included with a continuous probability that saturates to 0 or 1 as the weights push the configuration away from predicate ties. Gradients flow through the vertex weights and positions via the smooth relaxation, and sharpening toward a discrete tessellation is controlled by a temperature parameter on the relaxation.

The cost comes in three forms. First, the tessellation has to be **recomputed on every geometry update**, because the underlying weighted Delaunay configuration is a global function of all vertex positions and weights; there is no local-update shortcut analogous to mesh-refinement kernels. Second, the **temperature schedule has to be tuned** against the downstream loss — too sharp and the gradient vanishes at predicate ties, too soft and the tessellation is degenerate. Third, the method is a **surface triangulator**, not a volumetric tet mesher, and extending weighted Delaunay's probabilistic-face-inclusion to 3D tet meshes multiplies the predicate count substantially.

## Son 2024 (DMesh) — sigmoid-probability over a weighted Delaunay tessellation

Son, Gadelha, Zhou, Xu, Lin & Zhou 2024 (*DMesh*, NeurIPS 2024) refines the Rakotosaona direction. The representation stores vertex positions and per-vertex weights in the weighted Delaunay sense; face selection is a sigmoid of the power-diagram dual predicate, producing a per-face inclusion probability that is differentiable in the vertex weights. The learned representation is compact — one vector of positions and one vector of weights — and supports both surface and volumetric meshes conceptually.

DMesh's architectural advantage over Rakotosaona is the use of the geometric power-diagram dual rather than a purely-learned relaxation; the dual predicate has closed-form derivatives, so the relaxation temperature plays a smaller role and the gradient signal is cleaner. The cost picture is similar in shape: the weighted-Delaunay tessellation remains a global function of all vertex positions and weights, and re-tessellation per update dominates the compute at higher vertex counts. DMesh's follow-on (DMesh++) eliminates the weighted-Delaunay step entirely to address the scaling ceiling; the book treats the follow-on as evidence that the global-retessellation cost is load-bearing for DMesh itself rather than as a drop-in FEM candidate.

For `sim-soft` the scaling ceiling is binding. The canonical problem targets 30k tets and the full design-mode scene can reach 50k+; re-running a global weighted-Delaunay tessellation on every design edit is incompatible with the 50 ms parameter-only edit budget from [Part 7 Ch 04](../../70-sdf-pipeline/04-live-remesh.md). Production deployment of any soft-relaxation Delaunay approach in volumetric FEM has not been demonstrated in the published literature the book has surveyed.

## DMTet — fixed-topology tet grid with rare boundary events

[Shen et al. 2021](../../appendices/00-references/03-diff-sim.md#shen-2021) takes the opposite route. A *background* tetrahedral grid is pre-computed (the canonical choice is a regular grid of cubes, each subdivided into six tets); the SDF values are stored at grid vertices and the iso-surface is extracted by a differentiable marching-tetrahedra variant. Each background tet has only three canonical iso-surface topologies up to rotation (derived from the $2^4 = 16$ sign configurations on its four vertices, reduced by rotational symmetry), and the topology choice changes only when a grid vertex's SDF value crosses zero — which happens rarely across design edits and never at the interior of a cell.

The gradient flow through DMTet is clean wherever topology is fixed: SDF values and grid-vertex displacements propagate smoothly to iso-surface vertex positions. Topology events — a grid vertex crossing zero — are still discontinuities, but they are localized to a small fraction of grid cells and can be detected and handled separately. DMTet's production adoption in NVIDIA [GET3D](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.11163) (Gao et al., NeurIPS 2022) and [Magic3D](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.10440) (Lin et al., CVPR 2023) comes from this combination: the vast majority of gradient evaluations see fixed topology, and the occasional topology event is handled by a coarse enough annealing schedule that the downstream renderer tolerates the transient.

For `sim-soft` three problems remain. First, **DMTet's output is a surface triangle mesh**, not a volumetric tet mesh. The background tet grid is internal scaffolding; what gets exported is the iso-surface triangles. An FEM solver needs the enclosed volume tetrahedralized with good aspect ratio and material continuity, and DMTet does not emit that. Second, the **background tet scaffold scales cubically with grid resolution**, and the resolution must be high enough to capture surface features — so resolving a canonical-problem cavity at 30k output FEM tets under a hypothetical volumetric DMTet variant would inflate the scaffold tet count well past the FEM solver's budget. Third, **the phantom-element concern from [§01](01-diff-mc.md) returns at topology transitions** in attenuated form: when a grid vertex crosses zero, the iso-surface topology of the containing cells changes discontinuously, and the mesh at the transition step has vertices whose FEM mass and connectivity are not stably defined.

[Part 12 Ch 07's Option A](../../120-roadmap/07-open-questions.md) names the research direction that would close this gap: a volumetric DMTet variant that emits a full tet mesh of the enclosed region, with phantom-element weight-scaling at transitions absorbing the discontinuity. It is multi-year research, not a current deployment option.

## What this sub-leaf commits the book to

- **Rakotosaona 2021 is the soft-relaxation Delaunay baseline.** Per-vertex weights plus a learned temperature produce a differentiable weighted-Delaunay connectivity; re-tessellation on every geometry update is the scaling bottleneck.
- **Son 2024 (DMesh) is the closed-form-predicate refinement of the same line.** The sigmoid-over-power-diagram formulation cleans up the gradient signal but does not beat the global-retessellation scaling.
- **DMTet is the closest to production-ready of the differentiable-meshing approaches — for surface meshing, not volumetric.** The production pipelines that consume it (GET3D, Magic3D) use it as a surface mesher feeding a differentiable renderer; the FEM use case requires a volumetric extension that remains open research.
- **No differentiable-Delaunay or differentiable-tet approach currently ships a 30k-tet volumetric FEM-ready mesh with live gradient flow through topology changes.** The `sim-soft` compromise is [§03 FD wrappers at topology boundaries](03-fd-wrappers.md) inside the three-part framework of the [parent chapter](../05-diff-meshing.md).
