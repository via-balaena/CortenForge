# The research frontier

The [FD wrappers of §03](03-fd-wrappers.md) are a workable compromise inside the low-tens-of-parameters regime, but they are a compromise — the `sim-soft` commitment to differentiability-is-first-class has a known, flagged, bounded exception at the SDF boundary. Two research directions, either of which would close that exception, are currently active enough to plausibly land inside this book's post-Phase-I horizon without being committed to any Phase A–I schedule. This sub-leaf names both options, writes out the open questions each one carries, and grounds the architectural commitment the book is making in the meantime.

## Option A — volumetric DMTet for full tet-mesh FEM

The most direct path extends [DMTet](../../appendices/00-references/03-diff-sim.md#shen-2021) from surface iso-surface extraction ([§02](02-diff-delaunay.md)) to full volumetric tet-mesh output. The background tet grid already exists in DMTet's internals; what is missing is the emission of the *enclosed* tets (those where all four vertices have negative SDF) and the partial-tet treatment at the iso-surface (where one, two, or three vertices are positive). Two technical problems stand between the current DMTet and the volumetric variant:

- **Phantom-element weight scaling at topology transitions.** When an iso-surface crosses a grid edge, the topology of affected cells changes; DMTet's smooth vertex-position gradient breaks at that event. The natural fix — weighting the phantom elements by a smooth factor of the SDF value at the boundary, so their FEM contribution fades continuously to zero as they approach the boundary — is mathematically clean for the forward evaluation but introduces instabilities in the IPC barrier ([Ch 01 §02](../01-custom-vjps/02-contact-barrier.md)): a tet with weight approaching zero has arbitrary geometric predicates, which the IPC CCD kernel cannot cope with. The stabilization strategy is an open question — barrier-regularization + weight-floor is one candidate; excluding near-zero-weight tets from contact but keeping them in elasticity is another. Neither has been demonstrated.
- **Learned-parameter integration with the rest of the RL loop.** Volumetric DMTet would introduce a *learned* parameter (the annealing temperature on the background-grid SDF values). The [Ch 01 §00 registration API](../../60-differentiability/01-custom-vjps/00-registration.md)'s arbitrary-fan-in input slot already accommodates an additional learned parameter mechanically — it is just one more `Var` in `inputs` — but the training-loop side is open: the temperature's gradient signal comes from the downstream FEM reward, and whether that signal is informative enough to drive temperature annealing (rather than being drowned out by the FEM-loss variance) is not known without a Phase-specific benchmark.

Open questions the book flags:

1. Do the phantom elements destabilize the IPC barrier at topology-transition events? The worry is that near-zero-weight tets with arbitrary geometric predicates feed the CCD kernel inputs it does not handle cleanly.
2. Does the annealing schedule compose with the [Revolve checkpointing](../04-checkpointing/01-revolve.md) that Phase E may adopt? A learned temperature that changes across the trajectory would complicate Revolve's primal-only checkpoint payload.
3. Can the learned temperature be *prescribed* rather than learned — an explicit schedule as a function of optimizer progress — avoiding the open question of whether the FEM-reward gradient signal is informative enough to drive annealing directly?

Any of these is a separate multi-month research engagement. The book treats Option A as a scheduled-work direction with unknown timeline, not a committed deliverable.

## Option B — topology-aware gradient via an analytically-integrated Dirac adjoint

The second direction attacks the topology-change gradient rather than the topology representation. The Dirac-delta contribution [§00](00-why-not-mc.md) named at a topology-change boundary is *not* a bug; it encodes the correct infinitesimal change-of-configuration-space information, and integrating it in a Monte-Carlo estimator would yield an unbiased gradient estimate at the boundary itself.

The research analog is **differentiable Monte-Carlo rendering** — specifically the boundary-integral treatment of visibility discontinuities. [Li et al. 2018](../../appendices/00-references/04-rendering.md#li-2018) formulates the pixel-colour derivative as an interior smooth integral plus an explicit boundary integral along silhouette edges, where the boundary integral absorbs the Dirac contribution from the discontinuous visibility function. [Bangaru et al. 2020](../../appendices/00-references/04-rendering.md#bangaru-2020) generalizes that construction to an unbiased estimator via a warped-area reparameterization that converts the edge boundary integral into an area integral, removing the explicit-edge-detection step. Both papers ship working implementations for differentiable rendering.

The analogy to FEM topology changes is the research hypothesis. A Dirac-delta contribution at a mesh-topology-change boundary is structurally the same object: a step discontinuity in a multi-dimensional function whose derivative is a distribution supported on a codimension-1 manifold. If the topology-change boundary in SDF-parameter space can be parameterized explicitly, and if the Dirac's coefficient can be derived from the SDF geometry at the crossing, a Monte-Carlo estimator that samples along the boundary manifold gives an unbiased gradient — no relaxation, no smoothing, no FD noise.

Three open questions stand between the rendering analog and a usable FEM gradient:

1. **What is the Dirac coefficient for a mesh-topology change?** In rendering it is the visibility jump times the incident radiance; in FEM it is the change in the energy functional across the topology boundary, times the rate at which the mesh crosses that boundary in parameter space. Writing the coefficient explicitly requires a clean definition of "the mesh energy at the boundary," which in turn requires deciding what the state transfer between the two topologies is at the boundary point. The [Part 7 Ch 04 §02 state-transfer machinery](../../70-sdf-pipeline/04-live-remesh/02-state-transfer.md) is the natural candidate; formalizing its limit as the parameter approaches the boundary is open work.
2. **Can the boundary manifold be sampled efficiently?** The differentiable-rendering line sampling is efficient because silhouette edges are sub-linear in scene complexity and admit spatial hierarchies. A mesh-topology boundary in SDF-parameter space is a $(n_\theta - 1)$-dimensional manifold in an $n_\theta$-dimensional space, and its complexity scales with the SDF's own zero-set structure. Efficient sampling likely requires an adaptive hierarchy over SDF parameters — structurally similar to [Bangaru 2020](../../appendices/00-references/04-rendering.md#bangaru-2020)'s warped-area reparameterization, but the geometric primitives are different.
3. **Does the estimator integrate with the existing [time adjoint](../03-time-adjoint.md) and [IFT](../02-implicit-function.md) machinery?** The Dirac-boundary estimator contributes a boundary integral that is evaluated directly at the crossing rather than propagated by a differential equation; the forward time adjoint and IFT produce contributions that are propagated. Composing them requires knowing the time-adjoint's behaviour across a topology transition, which again needs a formalization of state-transfer-at-the-limit.

Porting the visibility-boundary treatment from rendering to FEM topology changes has not been published for volumetric FEM, to the book's knowledge. The work is plausibly feasible — the mathematical structure is the same, the FEM community has absorbed adjoint-method formalism deeply, and the rendering-side implementations are well-documented. It is not plausibly *easy*. The book flags it as the sharper of the two research directions, in the sense that a clean solution would obviate both the FD wrapper and DMTet's phantom-element issue simultaneously.

## What the book commits to in the meantime

Both options are multi-year research engagements. The book commits to neither in Phase A–I. What the book does commit to is the architectural posture that makes either option a *drop-in extension* rather than a rewrite:

- **The [chassis VJP registration API](../01-custom-vjps/00-registration.md) is extensible.** A volumetric-DMTet VJP (Option A) or a boundary-integral VJP (Option B) both land as new `CustomVjp` implementations on the existing `register_custom_vjp` surface. No chassis refactor.
- **The [`GradientEstimate` handshake](../../appendices/01-glossary.md#gradientestimatenoisy-variance) is variance-aware.** A successful Option B deployment returns `Exact` on topology crossings where the FD wrapper previously returned `Noisy { variance }`; the outer optimizer sees the transition as a noise-floor reduction, not as an API change.
- **The [topology-fixed-per-episode framework](../05-diff-meshing.md) is option-agnostic.** Inside an episode, gradients flow through fixed-topology IFT and time-adjoint; between episodes, state transfer and re-mesh happen. Whichever research option closes the topology-crossing gap, the episode framework around it is unchanged.

This is the architecture the book is defending. Differentiability-as-first-class is preserved inside episodes; the gap between episodes is *contained* at the FD-wrapper boundary and flagged at the API surface. A research move closes the gap by replacing the FD wrapper at its current location; a research stall leaves the compromise in place. Either way, the rest of the book's differentiability commitment is trustworthy precisely because this chapter has named its limit.

## What this sub-leaf commits the book to

- **Option A — volumetric DMTet — is the more incremental research path.** Open questions include IPC-barrier stability under near-zero-weight phantom elements, composition of an annealing schedule with [Revolve checkpointing](../04-checkpointing/01-revolve.md), and whether the FEM-reward gradient signal is informative enough to drive temperature annealing directly (or whether the temperature is better prescribed as a schedule of optimizer progress).
- **Option B — topology-aware Dirac adjoint — is the sharper research path.** Porting the [Li 2018](../../appendices/00-references/04-rendering.md#li-2018) / [Bangaru 2020](../../appendices/00-references/04-rendering.md#bangaru-2020) visibility-boundary treatment to FEM topology changes is unpublished for volumetric FEM to the book's knowledge; the mathematical structure is analogous, the open questions are about state-transfer limits and efficient boundary-manifold sampling.
- **The book commits to neither option in Phase A–I but leaves both as drop-in extensions** against the chassis VJP registration surface and the `GradientEstimate` handshake.
- **The architectural posture is option-agnostic**: a closed-gap future (Option A or B) replaces the FD wrapper in place; a stalled-research future keeps the compromise named and flagged. The commitment to differentiability-as-first-class everywhere *except through the SDF* is honest at either endpoint.
