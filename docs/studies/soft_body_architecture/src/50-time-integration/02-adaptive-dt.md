# Adaptive timestep

Backward Euler is unconditionally stable — any $\Delta t$ yields a bounded solution — but not arbitrarily *accurate*. Too-large $\Delta t$ degrades contact detection (fast-moving pairs skip over each other between steps), inflates Newton's inner iteration count, and trips the [line-search CCD clip](00-backward-euler/02-line-search.md) repeatedly, which is itself the primary signal that $\Delta t$ is too large. The adaptive-timestep layer catches this and shrinks. There is no fixed-$\Delta t$ mode in `sim-soft`'s production path; the user provides a *target* $\Delta t$ (e.g., 16 ms for 60 FPS experience mode, 1 ms for design-mode precision) and a *minimum* floor, and the solver shrinks within that range when it needs to.

| Section | What it covers |
|---|---|
| [Energy conservation monitors](02-adaptive-dt/00-energy.md) | Backstop: total-energy-plus-dissipation tracking across steps; triggers a retry-with-halved-$\Delta t$ if step-over-step energy drift exceeds a tolerance. Off by default in production — primary path is CCD-driven |
| [CCD-driven shrinking](02-adaptive-dt/01-ccd-shrink.md) | Primary path: when [Newton's line search](00-backward-euler/02-line-search.md) reports repeated CCD clips or fails entirely, the outer loop halves $\Delta t$ and restarts the step. Shrink triggers are deterministic and test-reproducible |

Three claims this chapter rests on.

**CCD-driven shrink is the primary mechanism, energy-monitor is the backstop.** The [IPC CCD](../40-contact/01-ipc-internals/02-ccd.md) gives an exact time-of-impact for every active contact pair along a proposed Newton step. When the smallest toi forces the line search below an effective step length (empirically $\alpha < 2^{-8} \approx 0.004$ after 8 halvings), the Newton iteration cannot make useful progress and the outer loop halves $\Delta t$. This is the deterministic, replayable path — every shrink has a specific contact pair blaming it, visible in diagnostic logs. Energy drift is a softer signal that matters for debugging (it surfaces constitutive-law bugs and autograd-tape errors as conservation violations) but is too noisy in the presence of contact friction and viscoelastic dissipation to drive production shrinking.

**Shrink and grow are asymmetric on purpose.** Halving on failure is cheap (re-run one step at $\Delta t/2$); doubling on success is expensive in the worst case (overshoot $\Delta t$ back into the CCD-clipping regime and pay the halving cost plus a wasted full step). Production `sim-soft` shrinks on any failure and grows only after $N$ consecutive successful steps at the current $\Delta t$ (default $N = 8$). The asymmetry is the adaptive-timestep literature's standard (Söderlind's "PI controller" for ODE solvers, Hairer–Wanner's safety factors), specialized to the contact-dominated regime where a single popped constraint costs more than eight slightly-slow steps.

**The adaptive layer does not interact with the autograd tape — in Phase D through G.** A step that is retried at smaller $\Delta t$ records only the *successful* retry on the tape — the failed attempts are discarded. [Part 6 Ch 02's IFT adjoint](../60-differentiability/02-implicit-function.md) therefore sees a clean sequence of converged Newton steps at (possibly varying) $\Delta t$ values, one factorization per step. The decision-to-shrink is treated as a non-smooth discrete event and is not differentiated. When a design parameter meaningfully affects *which* steps trigger shrinks, the current fallback is an FD wrapper with [`GradientEstimate::Noisy { variance }`](../60-differentiability/05-diff-meshing.md) flagging downstream — the same compromise [Part 6 Ch 05's diff-meshing chapter](../60-differentiability/05-diff-meshing.md) names for mesh-topology changes.

**Deferred to Phase H: stochastic adjoints over adaptive shrinks.** Replacing the FD wrapper with a proper stochastic-adjoint treatment — differentiating the *expected* trajectory over the shrink-decision distribution — is scheduled alongside the other stochastic-adjoint work in Phase H (viscoelastic time-axis, thermal coupling, per Part 6 Ch 03). The deferral is explicit and tied to a phase, not indefinite; the fallback works in Phases D–G because design-mode sweeps rarely cross shrink boundaries, and when they do the noise is flagged rather than silently biased.

## Adaptive-timestep alternatives not pursued

**Runge-Kutta-Fehlberg error estimation** (embedded higher-order schemes that estimate local truncation error). Works for ODE IVPs; does not extend cleanly to the contact-barrier-dominated regime where accuracy is limited by contact geometry, not by elasticity smoothness. Rejected.

**PI/PID step controllers** on a computed error norm. Smoother than halve-on-failure but adds controller-tuning knobs that interact with material stiffness, contact density, and friction coefficient in ways that are hard to calibrate. The halve-on-failure scheme is uglier but self-tuning within its specified range.
