# Tet4 — linear tetrahedra

The [element-choice parent](../00-element-choice.md) named Tet4 as the Phase B default. This leaf writes the Tet4 element down — its 4 nodes, its linear shape functions, its constant deformation gradient, the 12×12 per-element stiffness it produces — and identifies which downstream chapters consume which Tet4 properties. Volumetric locking and rim-deformation inaccuracy (the two well-documented Tet4 failure modes the parent named) are not re-derived here; they are addressed at the material layer in [Ch 05 incompressibility](../../../20-materials/05-incompressibility.md) and at the element layer by [Tet10](01-tet10.md), respectively.

## Reference geometry and shape functions

A Tet4 element has 4 nodes labeled $i = 1, \ldots, 4$ at positions $X_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$ in the reference configuration. Its shape functions $N_i(\xi, \eta, \zeta)$ in barycentric coordinates $(\xi, \eta, \zeta)$ on the reference tetrahedron are the four affine functions that take value 1 at node $i$ and 0 at the other three:

$$ N_1 = 1 - \xi - \eta - \zeta, \quad N_2 = \xi, \quad N_3 = \eta, \quad N_4 = \zeta $$

Each $N_i$ is linear in $(\xi, \eta, \zeta)$, so its gradient $\nabla_X N_i$ is constant within the element — this is the property that makes everything downstream constant per tet.

A point inside the tet maps from reference to current configuration by the displacement-interpolation $u(X) = \sum_i N_i(X)\, u_i$, with the $u_i$ the per-node displacement vectors. The element's deformation gradient is then:

$$ F = I + \frac{\partial u}{\partial X} = I + \sum_{i=1}^{4} u_i \otimes \nabla_X N_i $$

Since each $\nabla_X N_i$ is constant on the element and the $u_i$ are per-node vectors (constant *for the element*), $F$ is **constant within each tet**. Strain $\varepsilon$, the right Cauchy-Green tensor $C = F^T F$, the volume ratio $J = \det F$, the [invariants $I_1, I_2, I_3$](../../../20-materials/01-strain-measures/03-invariants.md), and the first-Piola stress $P(F)$ from the [`Material::tangent()`](../../../20-materials/00-trait-hierarchy/00-trait-surface.md) call are all constant per tet.

## Per-element stiffness assembly

The element's degrees of freedom are the 4 nodal displacements $u_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$, totaling 12 DOFs. The element stiffness matrix $K^e$ is therefore $12 \times 12$, organized as a $4 \times 4$ block matrix of $3 \times 3$ blocks indexed by $(i, j)$. Each block is:

$$ K^e_{ij} = V^e \, \big(\nabla_X N_i\big)^T \, \mathbb{C}(F) \, \nabla_X N_j $$

with $V^e$ the reference-configuration volume of the tet and $\mathbb{C}(F) = \partial P / \partial F$ the material tangent stiffness from [Ch 04 — neo-Hookean tangent leaf](../../../20-materials/04-hyperelastic/00-neo-hookean/01-tangent.md). Because $F$, the $\nabla_X N_i$, and $\mathbb{C}(F)$ are all constant on the element, the integral collapses to one evaluation times the element volume — **no Gauss-point quadrature is needed**. The [`Material::tangent()`](../../../20-materials/00-trait-hierarchy/00-trait-surface.md) trait method is called exactly once per Tet4 element, not once per quadrature point.

The internal force vector $f^e_\text{int}$ — the residual contribution Newton iterates against in [Ch 00 backward-Euler](../../../50-time-integration/00-backward-euler.md) — has the same structure:

$$ f^e_{\text{int},\,i} = V^e \, P(F) \, \nabla_X N_i $$

a $4 \times 3$ block of values, one per node, evaluated from the single $P(F)$ call.

## Cost profile

Per-element work for one Newton residual + tangent evaluation, in `f64` floating-point operations:

- Compute $F$ from current node positions: $4 \times 3 \times 3 = 36$ FMAs (outer-product accumulation)
- Compute $J = \det F$: 9 multiplies + 5 adds
- Compute $F^{-T}$ (cached for $P$ and $\mathbb{C}$): one $3 \times 3$ matrix inverse, ≈30 ops
- One `Material::tangent()` call returning $(P, \mathbb{C})$
- Assemble $K^e$ ($16$ blocks of $3 \times 3$, each from outer-products with the precomputed $\nabla_X N_i$): ≈400–800 ops depending on $\mathbb{C}$ representation
- Assemble $f^e_\text{int}$: 4 blocks × 9 ops = 36 ops

Total ≈1500 FLOPs per Tet4 per Newton step plus the constitutive-law cost. Tet4 with neo-Hookean is the cheapest hyperelastic per-element evaluation `sim-soft` ships; the [tradeoff sub-leaf](04-tradeoff.md) puts numbers on per-DOF and per-FLOP comparisons against Tet10 and Hex8.

## Sparsity contribution

Each Tet4 element contributes a $12 \times 12$ block to the global stiffness matrix, scattered into the 4 node rows × 4 node columns. After assembly, a node's row in the global block sparsity has one $3 \times 3$ nonzero block per neighbor vertex (vertices it shares an edge with through any incident tet). The [Part 8 Ch 01 sparse-matrix BSR-3 layout](../../../80-gpu/01-sparse-matrix/01-bsr.md) is sized for exactly this regime; Tet10's denser sparsity pattern (the corner-vertex row also picks up the edge-midpoint nodes of every incident tet) sits on the same BSR-3 structure with no layout change, only a row-pointer growth. The specific average valence on a given mesh is a [Ch 01 mesh-quality](../../01-mesh-quality.md) property of the generator and post-improvement passes, not a Tet4 property.

## Volumetric locking and the rim failure — pointers, not derivations

Tet4's constant deformation gradient is what makes assembly cheap and what makes the element fail under near-incompressibility and on bent contact regions. **Volumetric locking** under $\nu \approx 0.49$ silicone is a [Ch 05 sub-leaf](../../../20-materials/05-incompressibility/00-locking.md) topic; the constant-$F$ assumption forces the volumetric constraint to be enforced too stiffly across the entire element, the element resists volume change beyond what the constitutive law would dictate at a Gauss point, and the mesh visibly under-deforms. **Rim-deformation inaccuracy** is the [Part 1 Ch 02 sub-leaf](../../../10-physical/02-what-goes-wrong/04-rim.md) failure mode — a tet bending across a contact edge cannot represent the bending mode within its constant-$F$ kinematics; the deformation distributes incorrectly across the element's interior. Both failures are real, both ship in Phase D's deliverable, and both are addressed in Phase H — locking by [mixed u-p](../../../20-materials/05-incompressibility/01-mixed-up.md), rim by [Tet10](01-tet10.md).

## What this sub-leaf commits the book to

- **One `Material::tangent()` call per Tet4 per Newton step.** Not per quadrature point. The hot-path call-site signature in the `element/` module ([Part 11 Ch 00 element](../../../110-crate/00-module-layout/01-element.md)) is the per-element pattern, and Tet10's per-Gauss-point pattern uses the same trait through a different call-site.
- **Per-element stiffness is exactly $12 \times 12$.** Sparse-Hessian assembly ([Part 8 Ch 01 BSR](../../../80-gpu/01-sparse-matrix/01-bsr.md)) treats this as a $4 \times 4$ block of $3 \times 3$ blocks; the BSR-3 block size is tied to vertex DOFs, not element type.
- **No quadrature for Tet4.** The integration weight is $V^e$ alone; the element-quadrature abstraction the [Tet10 sub-leaf](01-tet10.md) introduces is bypassed for Tet4 via a one-point degenerate quadrature with weight $V^e$ at the centroid. The same trait surface holds; the per-element call counts differ.
- **Tet4-inherited deformation errors are Phase H concerns.** The [first-working milestone](../../../120-roadmap/02-first-working.md) ships Tet4-only; the [gradcheck pass](../../../120-roadmap/02-first-working/01-gradcheck.md) certifies differentiability of the Tet4 path, not visual fidelity on rims or under near-incompressibility. Visual-fidelity acceptance is Phase H, post-Tet10 + post-mixed-u-p.
