# Tet10 — quadratic tetrahedra

The [element-choice parent](../00-element-choice.md) named Tet10 as the Phase H upgrade, with the rationale "rim deformation demands it". This leaf writes Tet10 down — its 10 nodes, its quadratic shape functions, the 4-point Gauss quadrature it needs, the 30×30 per-element stiffness matrix it produces — and identifies the call-site differences from the [Tet4 sibling](00-tet4.md) that a single trait surface has to span. Mixed-element handling at Tet4↔Tet10 interfaces is deferred to the [mixed sub-leaf](03-mixed.md).

## Reference geometry and shape functions

A Tet10 element has 10 nodes: 4 corner nodes (same as Tet4) plus 6 edge-midpoint nodes (one per edge of the tetrahedron). Its shape functions $N_i(\xi, \eta, \zeta)$ are quadratic in the barycentric coordinates $(\xi, \eta, \zeta)$ on the reference tetrahedron, with $\xi_4 := 1 - \xi - \eta - \zeta$ for the fourth corner. The standard form is:

$$ N_1 = \xi_4(2\xi_4 - 1), \quad N_2 = \xi(2\xi - 1), \quad N_3 = \eta(2\eta - 1), \quad N_4 = \zeta(2\zeta - 1) $$

$$ N_5 = 4\xi_4 \xi, \quad N_6 = 4\xi \eta, \quad N_7 = 4\xi_4 \eta, \quad N_8 = 4\xi_4 \zeta, \quad N_9 = 4\xi \zeta, \quad N_{10} = 4\eta \zeta $$

Each corner $N_i$ vanishes at the other three corners and at all six midpoints; each midpoint $N_i$ takes value 1 at its midpoint and 0 at every corner and the other five midpoints. The corner shape functions are quadratic with a single bowed peak; the midpoint shape functions are products of two linear barycentric coordinates (e.g., $4\xi_4\xi$), giving a quadratic profile that peaks at value 1 at the corresponding midpoint.

The displacement interpolation is $u(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{10} N_i(X)\, u_i$. The deformation gradient $F = I + \sum_i u_i \otimes \nabla_X N_i$ is **linear in $(\xi, \eta, \zeta)$** because each $\nabla_X N_i$ is linear in barycentric coordinates. Strain, stress, and the [tangent stiffness $\mathbb{C}(F)$](../../../20-materials/04-hyperelastic/00-neo-hookean/01-tangent.md) therefore vary linearly across the element rather than being constant — this is the Tet4 → Tet10 step that lets a single element represent bending modes.

## Gauss-point quadrature

Because $F$ is non-constant, the per-element integral that gives $K^e$ and $f^e_\text{int}$ no longer collapses to one evaluation. Tet10's quadratic shape functions have linear gradients $\nabla_X N_i$, so the linear-elastic stiffness integrand $(\nabla_X N_i)^T \mathbb{C}\, \nabla_X N_j$ is quadratic in $(\xi, \eta, \zeta)$ when $\mathbb{C}$ is constant. The 4-point symmetric Gauss rule on the reference tetrahedron has degree-of-precision 2 — exact for the linear-elastic stiffness integrand, and to engineering accuracy for the nonlinear-constitutive case where $\mathbb{C}(F)$ varies slowly across the element. Quadrature nodes are at the symmetric Stroud points with equal weights $w_q = V^e / 4$.

The per-element work pattern is:

1. For each of the 4 Gauss points $q$, evaluate $F^q$ from the 10 nodal positions and the precomputed $\nabla_X N_i^q$.
2. Call [`Material::tangent()`](../../../20-materials/00-trait-hierarchy/00-trait-surface.md) once per Gauss point, returning $(P^q, \mathbb{C}^q)$.
3. Accumulate the stiffness contributions $K^e_{ij} \mathrel{+}= w_q\, (\nabla_X N_i^q)^T\, \mathbb{C}^q\, \nabla_X N_j^q$ across the 4 Gauss points.
4. Accumulate $f^e_{\text{int},i} \mathrel{+}= w_q\, P^q\, \nabla_X N_i^q$ similarly.

The constitutive call count is therefore **4 per Tet10 element per Newton step** versus 1 per Tet4. This call-count multiplier is the dominant Tet4 → Tet10 cost driver when the constitutive law is expensive (Ogden, HGO); when the law is cheap (neo-Hookean), the assembly arithmetic dominates and the multiplier is closer to 4× total work, not 4× call cost.

## Per-element stiffness and DOF count

Tet10 has 30 DOFs per element (10 nodes × 3). The element stiffness matrix $K^e$ is therefore $30 \times 30$, organized as a $10 \times 10$ block matrix of $3 \times 3$ blocks. The block sparsity is fully populated at the element level (every Tet10 node-pair shares the element), so all 100 blocks are nonzero at element-assembly time. After global assembly, the row-density per node is higher than for a Tet4 mesh because each corner node is now connected — through any incident Tet10 — to all 9 other nodes of every incident element (3 other corners + 6 midpoints), and each midpoint node is connected to all 9 other nodes of its incident elements as well.

The [Part 8 Ch 01 BSR-3 layout](../../../80-gpu/01-sparse-matrix/01-bsr.md) accepts this directly — the block size $3 \times 3$ is keyed to vertex DOFs, not element type — and the only change from a Tet4 mesh is a row-pointer growth. The [Part 11 Ch 04 testing strategy](../../../110-crate/04-testing.md) treats Tet10 as a separate regression baseline rather than a Tet4-extension because the larger basis admits deformation modes Tet4's regression suite does not exercise.

## Cost comparison to Tet4 — the 3–5× factor

The "≈3–5× per-element cost of Tet4" claim from the [parent's Tet10 section](../00-element-choice.md) decomposes as follows:

- **Constitutive calls per element: 4× (4 Gauss points vs 1 centroid evaluation).** Dominant when the law is expensive.
- **Per-element assembly arithmetic: ≈6× (100 blocks vs 16 blocks, with the contraction-with-$\mathbb{C}$ cost identical per block).** Dominant when the law is cheap.
- **Sparse-Hessian nonzero count: ≈2.5× (more nodes per element, more cross-coupling).** Dominant in the linear-solve cost downstream from assembly.

The aggregate 3–5× wall-clock factor is workload-dependent. The [tradeoff sub-leaf](04-tradeoff.md) puts numbers on the canonical-problem regime where these factors compose; the takeaway from the parent's Phase H commitment is that the rim-deformation fidelity gain in the contact band justifies the 3–5× contact-band cost when the bulk stays Tet4 (the [mixed-element pattern](03-mixed.md)).

## Material assignment at Gauss points, not centroids

The Tet4 evaluation path samples the [per-element material parameters from Part 7 Ch 02](../../../70-sdf-pipeline/02-material-assignment.md) at the centroid; one parameter set per element. The Tet10 evaluation path samples per Gauss point — the [sampling sub-leaf](../../../70-sdf-pipeline/02-material-assignment/00-sampling.md) calls this out as the call-site difference, with the Phase H Tet10-in-band commitment requiring the SDF-valued field to be evaluable at arbitrary positions inside the element rather than at element-tagged centroids only. The [`Material` trait](../../../20-materials/00-trait-hierarchy/00-trait-surface.md) does not change; the field-sampling layer does.

This composes cleanly with the [stress-driven adaptive refinement from Part 7 Ch 03](../../../70-sdf-pipeline/03-adaptive-refine.md) because Tet10's refinement-trigger metric (stress-gradient at Gauss points) is already evaluated on the same quadrature grid the constitutive law uses; no extra evaluation is needed for refinement decisions.

## What this sub-leaf commits the book to

- **Tet10's `Material::tangent()` call count is 4 per element per Newton step.** Same trait, same signature; the call site is per-Gauss-point in the Tet10 element implementation versus per-element in the Tet4 implementation.
- **The 4-point Gauss rule is the Phase H default.** Reduced integration (1-point Tet10) is a known quadrature trick with implementation tradeoffs — locking-relief at the cost of hourglass-mode susceptibility — and is not shipped. The trade is not architecturally interesting at the [Part 11 Ch 00 element module](../../../110-crate/00-module-layout/01-element.md) level; it is a per-quadrature-rule tunable inside the Tet10 element impl.
- **Per-element stiffness is exactly $30 \times 30$.** The same BSR-3 block sparsity holds at the global level; row-pointer counts grow but block size and layout do not change.
- **Phase H ships Tet10-in-band, not globally Tet10.** This commitment binds the [Part 7 Ch 02 material-assignment sampling sub-leaf](../../../70-sdf-pipeline/02-material-assignment/00-sampling.md) to evaluate the per-element material parameters at Gauss points (not centroids) inside the Tet10-tagged region while keeping the Tet4-tagged bulk on centroid sampling.
- **Tet10 admits bending modes within a single element.** Quadratic shape functions allow $F$ to vary linearly across the element, which is what makes the [rim deformation failure of Tet4](../../../10-physical/02-what-goes-wrong/04-rim.md) recoverable in Tet10 without h-refining the rim mesh further.
