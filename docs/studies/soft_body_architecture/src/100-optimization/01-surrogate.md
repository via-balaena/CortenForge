# Surrogate models

A surrogate model is a cheap function $\widehat R(\theta)$ fit to a small number of expensive $R(\theta)$ evaluations, then queried in place of the real forward map wherever the optimizer's inner loops can tolerate approximation error. Surrogates are not a substitute for the forward map in [Ch 00](00-forward.md); they are an amortization layer that makes acquisition optimization, uncertainty estimation, and batch-selection tractable. This chapter names the three surrogate concerns `sim-soft` commits to and points at the sub-chapters that flesh each one.

`sim-soft`'s primary surrogate family is the Gaussian process, discussed as part of the [BayesOpt chapter](02-bayesopt.md). Neural surrogates live here because they are the scaling path when GPs saturate — $d \gtrsim 100$, $n \gtrsim 10^4$ — and because they carry a different uncertainty story that deserves its own treatment.

Three claims Ch 01 rests on:

1. **Surrogates do not replace the forward map — they replace the forward map in specific places.** Acquisition-function inner-loop searches, batch-BayesOpt look-ahead, and the Ch 04 active-learning information-gain computations all call the forward map thousands of times per outer step; substituting a 100 μs surrogate for a 50 ms real evaluation buys 500× in those places. The optimizer's *outer loop* always ground-truths with the real forward map, every step. Surrogate-only optimization is explicitly rejected — the reward-surface peculiarities in [Ch 00's landscape section](00-forward.md) include a heavy tail at the peak-pressure barrier that surrogates under-fit unless trained specifically for that region.
2. **Gradient-enhanced training is available because the forward map gives exact gradients on ≈95% of samples.** Per [Ch 00's 95/5 split](00-forward.md), most training samples arrive as $(\theta, R, \nabla_\theta R)$ triples. The training loss matches both the scalar and its gradient, yielding $d + 1$ supervision signals per sample instead of one — a substantial gain when $d$ is moderate (Wu et al. 2017 on gradient-enhanced GPs; Czarnecki et al. 2017 on "Sobolev training" for neural surrogates). On noisy-gradient samples, the gradient term is down-weighted by the reported variance.
3. **Uncertainty quantification is load-bearing, not a nice-to-have.** [Ch 02's BayesOpt](02-bayesopt.md) and [Ch 04's information-theoretic criteria](04-active-learning.md) both consume the surrogate's *posterior variance*, not just its mean. A surrogate that produces good point predictions and bad uncertainty breaks every acquisition function that uses it. Deep ensembles are the recommended baseline because they produce calibrated uncertainty on out-of-distribution $\theta$; single-network dropout surrogates are known to under-cover at the boundary of the training set. See [Guo et al. 2017](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.04599) on calibration more generally.
