# Gaussian processes

The parent spine's Claim 2 is that `sim-soft`'s BayesOpt surrogate is a *gradient-enhanced Gaussian process* with a Matérn-5/2 kernel and ARD length-scales per design axis. This leaf names why each of those four choices — GP prior, Matérn-5/2, ARD, gradient augmentation — is the one the parent commits to rather than the near-neighbor alternatives, and works through the posterior-inference machinery that the acquisition functions in [§01](01-acquisitions.md) consume.

## The GP prior — why over a neural surrogate in the BayesOpt loop

A Gaussian process places a Gaussian prior on the function $R(\theta)$ itself: at any finite set of points $\Theta = \{\theta_i\}_{i=1}^n$, the vector $(R(\theta_1), \dots, R(\theta_n))$ is jointly Gaussian with mean vector $\mu(\Theta)$ and covariance matrix $K(\Theta, \Theta)$ determined by a kernel function $k(\theta, \theta')$. Conditioning on observations gives a closed-form predictive distribution at a new $\theta_\ast$:

$$
\mu_\ast = k(\theta_\ast, \Theta) \, K^{-1} \mathbf R, \qquad
\sigma^2_\ast = k(\theta_\ast, \theta_\ast) - k(\theta_\ast, \Theta) \, K^{-1} k(\Theta, \theta_\ast).
$$

The closed-form posterior is the reason GPs are the default surrogate for BayesOpt at the evaluation budgets Part 10 targets: the acquisition functions in [§01](01-acquisitions.md) read $\mu_\ast$ and $\sigma^2_\ast$ directly, and both are computed from a single Cholesky factorization of $K$ that can be cached across all the inner-loop calls the acquisition optimizer issues. [Ch 01](../01-surrogate.md)'s neural-ensemble surrogate is the alternative for the $n \gtrsim 10^4$, $d \gtrsim 100$ regime where $O(n^3)$ inversion dominates; at BayesOpt's natural budget of $n \in [10^2, 10^3]$, one Cholesky-factored object serves posterior-mean, posterior-variance, and marginal-likelihood queries across the entire acquisition-optimization inner loop without needing the separately-trained $M$-member ensemble the neural path requires.

## Matérn-5/2 — the smoothness class that matches the physics

The kernel fixes the sample-path regularity of functions drawn from the prior. `sim-soft`'s reward $R(\theta)$ is $C^2$ within a topology per [Ch 00's reward-surface properties](../00-forward.md), so the prior's sample paths should match: a smoother kernel (squared-exponential, corresponding to $C^\infty$ sample paths) mis-identifies length-scales on a merely-twice-differentiable target because its smoothness prior is stronger than the data warrants; a rougher kernel (Matérn-1/2 or Matérn-3/2) under-uses the smoothness that is actually present.

Matérn-$\nu$ with $\nu = 5/2$ has sample-path regularity aligned with a $C^2$ target and admits a closed-form ARD extension:

$$
k_{5/2}(\theta, \theta') = \sigma_f^2 \left(1 + \sqrt{5}\, r + \tfrac{5}{3} r^2\right) \exp\!\left(-\sqrt{5}\, r\right),
\qquad
r^2 = \sum_{a=1}^d \frac{(\theta_a - \theta'_a)^2}{\ell_a^2}
$$

with signal variance $\sigma_f^2$ and per-dimension length-scales $\ell_a$. The combination of matched-regularity plus a closed-form ARD variant is why Matérn-5/2 is the common default in the BayesOpt literature — the regularity match is what earns it over squared-exponential here.

## ARD length-scales — why per-dimension rather than isotropic

Automatic Relevance Determination (ARD) fits one length-scale $\ell_a$ per design-space axis rather than a single isotropic $\ell$. The design vector $\theta$ from [Ch 00](../00-forward.md) mixes:

- SDF parameters measured in millimeters or radians,
- material parameters with orders-of-magnitude spread (stiffness in kPa-to-MPa, Prony time-constants in seconds),
- reward-composition weights in $[0, 1]$.

An isotropic kernel would either over-smooth in the small-range axes (washing out real variations) or under-smooth in the large-range axes (fitting noise). Per-dimension $\ell_a$ lets the kernel adapt: axes where $R$ varies rapidly get short $\ell_a$, axes where $R$ is near-constant get long $\ell_a$, and axes where $R$ is near-insensitive drift toward $\ell_a \to \infty$ and effectively drop out — automatic feature selection rather than manual.

Length-scales together with $\sigma_f^2$ and the nugget (below) are learned by maximizing the log marginal likelihood $\log p(\mathbf R \mid \Theta, \sigma_f^2, \{\ell_a\})$ over the training set. The marginal likelihood evaluation uses the Cholesky factor of $K$, so hyperparameter learning re-uses the same factorization the posterior prediction uses — no separate data path.

## The gradient-enhanced covariance

The forward map hands the optimizer $(\theta_i, R_i, \nabla_\theta R_i)$ for $\approx 95\%$ of samples per [Ch 00's split](../00-forward.md). A gradient-enhanced GP models the joint distribution over values *and* partials as a single Gaussian. Under a twice-differentiable kernel, the cross-covariances follow from differentiating $k$ directly. [Solak, Murray-Smith, Leithead, Leith & Rasmussen 2003](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper/2002/hash/5b8e4fd39d9786228649a8a8bec4e008-Abstract.html) develops this construction in the dynamic-systems context, augmenting the GP's covariance structure with value-derivative and derivative-derivative cross-covariances:

$$
\operatorname{Cov}\!\left(R(\theta),\ \frac{\partial R(\theta')}{\partial \theta'_a}\right) = \frac{\partial k(\theta, \theta')}{\partial \theta'_a},
\qquad
\operatorname{Cov}\!\left(\frac{\partial R(\theta)}{\partial \theta_a},\ \frac{\partial R(\theta')}{\partial \theta'_b}\right) = \frac{\partial^2 k(\theta, \theta')}{\partial \theta_a\, \partial \theta'_b}.
$$

Matérn-5/2 is twice differentiable everywhere, so both mixed partials are well-defined closed-form expressions. The augmented kernel matrix stacks the $n \times n$ value block, the value-derivative cross-block of shape $n \times nd$ (plus its transpose in the symmetric lower-triangle), and the $nd \times nd$ derivative-derivative block into a symmetric $n(d+1) \times n(d+1)$ system.

Ingesting $d$ gradient components per sample contributes $d$ additional supervision rows per sample; each of those rows constrains the posterior at a different derivative observation, not just at the function value. The practical payoff is that the posterior mean's *Jacobian* is informed by the training gradients directly, rather than having to be inferred from value-only observations at close-together points. [Wu, Poloczek, Wilson & Frazier 2017 (NeurIPS)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.04389) proposes the derivative-enabled Knowledge Gradient acquisition function on top of this kind of GP and reports state-of-the-art performance against both gradient-free and gradient-using baselines across synthetic and real-world benchmarks. The sample-efficiency intuition — each gradient-bearing observation supplies more information than a value-only one — is what makes this kind of GP the natural surrogate at the 95/5 gradient-availability split [Part 10 operates in](../00-forward.md).

## Posterior inference cost

The dominant cost is factorizing the augmented kernel matrix. For a budget of $n$ gradient-bearing samples in a $d$-dimensional design space, the augmented matrix is $n(d+1) \times n(d+1)$ and the Cholesky factorization is $O\big(n^3 (d+1)^3\big)$:

| $n$ | $d$ | Augmented matrix | Asymptotic Cholesky cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 100 | 20 | $2{,}100 \times 2{,}100$ | $\sim 10^{10}$ FLOPs |
| 500 | 20 | $10{,}500 \times 10{,}500$ | $\sim 10^{12}$ FLOPs |
| 1000 | 50 | $51{,}000 \times 51{,}000$ | $\sim 10^{14}$ FLOPs |

At $n = 500, d = 20$ — a typical BayesOpt budget on the canonical problem — the dense Cholesky is at the edge of single-machine tractability; block-structured dense factorization on `faer`'s `Llt<f64>` is what makes the factor-once, back-substitute-many pattern feasible within an interactive design loop. Past $n \gtrsim 2000$ even block-structured factorization becomes the dominant bottleneck, at which point the [Ch 01](../01-surrogate.md) neural-ensemble surrogate takes over for larger sample budgets.

Hyperparameter optimization re-factors $K$ once per gradient step on the marginal likelihood, so the factorization cost is paid several times per hyperparameter-update cycle — not once per outer BayesOpt iteration. Acquisition optimization, in contrast, calls back-substitution against the *cached* factor at every inner L-BFGS step; back-substitution is $O\big((n(d+1))^2\big)$, quadratic rather than cubic, which is what makes the acquisition-optimization inner loop affordable.

## The struct, with posterior prediction

```rust
use faer::sparse::linalg::solvers::Llt;
use sim_ml_chassis::Tensor;

pub struct GradientEnhancedGP {
    pub samples: Vec<GpSample>,
    pub length_scales: Tensor<f64>,       // ARD, one entry per design-space axis
    pub signal_variance: f64,             // sigma_f^2 in the kernel expression
    pub nugget: f64,                      // jitter added to the diagonal for conditioning
    pub factor: Option<Llt<f64>>,         // Cholesky factor of the augmented kernel, cached
    pub alpha: Option<Tensor<f64>>,       // K^{-1} y, cached for posterior-mean calls
}

pub struct GpSample {
    pub theta: Tensor<f64>,
    pub r: f64,
    pub grad_r: Option<Tensor<f64>>,
    pub grad_noise_var: Option<Tensor<f64>>,
}

pub struct GpPrediction {
    pub mean: f64,
    pub variance: f64,
}

impl GradientEnhancedGP {
    /// Posterior at theta*; re-uses the cached Cholesky factor.
    pub fn predict(&self, theta: &Tensor<f64>) -> GpPrediction { /* ... */ }

    /// Posterior-mean gradient; feeds the acquisition-function L-BFGS inner loop.
    pub fn predict_grad(&self, theta: &Tensor<f64>) -> Tensor<f64> { /* ... */ }
}
```

The `nugget` — a small positive value added to the augmented matrix's diagonal — is load-bearing for numerical conditioning: the derivative-observation blocks inflate the condition number of the augmented kernel relative to the value-only submatrix, and without jitter the Cholesky factor occasionally fails to complete on close-together samples. A nugget on the order of $10^{-6}\, \sigma_f^2$ is a common default; the exact value is tuned against the kernel matrix's observed condition number during hyperparameter learning.

The `alpha` $= K^{-1} y$ vector — where $y$ is the stacked values-and-gradients training vector — is cached alongside the factor. Posterior-mean evaluation is then a single matrix-vector product between the query-kernel row and $\alpha$; no per-query solve is needed. Posterior-variance evaluation does require a per-query back-substitution against the factor, so its cost is higher than posterior-mean evaluation by the quadratic factor above.

## What this sub-leaf commits the book to

- **Gaussian process with a Matérn-5/2 ARD kernel.** One length-scale per design-space axis plus a signal-variance scalar; hyperparameters fit by marginal-likelihood maximization.
- **Gradient augmentation via derivative kernels (Solak et al. 2003).** Value and partial-derivative observations go into a single $n(d+1) \times n(d+1)$ augmented kernel; Matérn-5/2's twice-differentiability makes the mixed partials well-defined. Wu et al. 2017 is the BayesOpt-side paper that uses this construction.
- **Cached `faer::sparse::linalg::solvers::Llt<f64>` factor** of the augmented kernel, reused across posterior-mean, posterior-variance, and marginal-likelihood evaluations. Same factor-once, back-substitute-many pattern as the Newton-Hessian factor-on-tape pattern from [Part 5 Ch 00](../../50-time-integration/00-backward-euler.md).
- **Nugget $\sim 10^{-6}\, \sigma_f^2$ for numerical conditioning.** Required because the derivative blocks inflate the augmented matrix's condition number relative to the value-only submatrix.
- **Tractability boundary near $n \gtrsim 2000$.** Past that point, [Ch 01](../01-surrogate.md)'s neural-ensemble surrogate takes over; the GP path is for the $n \in [10^2, 10^3]$ sample budget that BayesOpt inhabits.
