# What the loss numbers can and cannot say

> **Retraction, up front.** This study was motivated by the claim that roughly one seventh of food
> produced is lost between harvest and retail, and that mechanical damage is a large and
> under-attributed share of it. The first half is real. **The second half cannot be supported, and
> the specific mechanism proposed — that bruising initiates spoilage which then gets misattributed
> away from mechanical causes — was adversarially refuted.** The tonnage argument is retracted.
>
> This chapter is short because most of what it establishes is negative. If you are reading for the
> decision, the finding is in [Chapter 3](30-gap.md); this chapter exists so that nobody
> reconstructs the retracted argument later.

## The headline figure is real, and it is modelled

FAO's **Food Loss Index** (SDG indicator 12.3.1a): **13.3% globally in 2023**, up from 13.0% in
2015. Verified verbatim against the FAO SDG data portal.

Scope is routinely misquoted. The FLI covers on-farm, transport, storage, wholesale and processing
— it **excludes retail and consumer waste**, which are the separate UNEP Food Waste Index
(12.3.1b). Conflating them is how "one third of all food" enters conversation. The 0.3 pp movement
over eight years is inside model noise; what it says is that there has been **no progress** against
SDG 12.3's halving target.

**Fruits and vegetables are the worst commodity group at 25.4%** (2015: 23.2%), against roots,
tubers and oil-bearing crops at 12.3%, meat and animal products at 14.0%, and cereals and pulses at
8.4% — a 3.02× ratio to cereals. Regionally, sub-Saharan Africa is 23.0% against Northern America
and Europe at 10.0%.

> **Artefact caveat, load-bearing.** FAO's imputation model explicitly "imputes losses by food
> group, assuming loss levels are similar among products of a similar nature," using these exact
> clusters. The cross-commodity contrast is **partly a product of the model's own grouping
> structure**. It is *consistent with* the mechanical fragility of soft high-moisture produce. It is
> not *evidence for* it.

### It is an imputation, not a measurement

From FAO's own methodology working paper:

> "estimates for country-product pairs … can be very sensitive to changes in the method or the
> data, implying a high level of uncertainty on such detailed results, even at the country level.
> The results have proven more stable at the aggregated regional or global level … all results
> presented must be considered with caution."

Loss records were ~7% of reported FAOSTAT records over 1990–2016; only ~4.4% of country-year loss
observations are official reported data. The FLI is **not published at country level**, and
indicator 12.3.1 was originally classified Tier III.

Independent regimes disagree sharply. A 2025 field-measurement review (Ambuko et al., *Frontiers in
Horticulture*) finds a median whole-chain fruit-and-vegetable loss of **16.2%** against FAO-lineage
figures of 22–44%, attributing the gap to "assumptions made when modeling FLW estimates using
limited data points." Household survey data (LSMS-ISA) puts maize loss lower still. **Do not present
any single number as the truth** — and note that the maize figures carry no information about
mechanical damage in produce, since grain loss is storage and pest driven.

Full figures, regional tables, sample sizes and the survey-methodology caveats are in the
[appendix](appendices.md#claim-ledger).

## ★ The pivotal negative finding: there is no cause axis

Verified 3-0 by direct extraction of the full 52-page FAO working paper.

> **FAO's model contains no physical-cause attribution axis. A mechanical-damage share cannot be
> extracted from it, because the model does not have one.**

Causes enter only through the HLPE micro/meso/macro *socio-economic* taxonomy. Mishandling is named
exactly once, as a micro-level cause — "the decision to harvest at a non-optimal moment or
mishandling of produce" — and **never as a model variable**. The enumerated variable list is
exclusively socio-economic, price, and infrastructure: energy and fertilizer prices, electricity
access, credit to agriculture, GDP, trade, education and extension spending, metal prices as a
storage proxy, and the World Bank Logistics Performance Index.

**No impact energy. No bruise incidence. No drop height. No vibration variable.**

Two qualifications. Weather and climate *do* enter via the World Bank Climate Knowledge Portal, so
"no physical causes" is strictly true for **mechanical** causes only. And FAO's separate Food Loss
and Waste Database (37,000+ data points, 700+ publications) *does* record narrative causes
including mechanical damage — but as a literature repository, not a model input. Whether it is
minable for a mechanical share is [open question 9](appendices.md#open-questions).

**Consequence:** any mechanical-damage share must be built bottom-up from primary literature.
Asking the headline statistics to decompose was asking a question the data structurally cannot
answer.

## ★ The coupling hypothesis: partially supported, strong form killed

The original argument: mechanical damage breaches the skin, admitting pathogens and spiking
respiration, so fruit damaged *mechanically* is ultimately recorded as lost to *spoilage*.

**What survived (3-0).** Controlled pendulum impacts produce a dose-dependent physiological
response, not merely cosmetic injury. Pomegranate cv. 'Helow' at 1.18 J and 2.29 J (n=24/group, 28
days at 5 °C and 22 °C): bruise area and volume "increased significantly" with impact level, and
respiration and ethylene were "significantly (p<0.05) dependent on bruising impact level, storage
temperature, and storage duration." Banana cv. Malindi (n=219): peak ethylene 3.85×10⁻³ mg/kg/h on
day 2 in high-impact (0.273 J) fruit at 22 °C.

**What was refuted (0-3 and 1-2).** The actual load-bearing claim — that impacted fruit is lost
through a *microbial* endpoint which cause-attribution would misclassify as spoilage. The source
papers do not support that reading. Also refuted, listed so they do not leak back in: a 14.02
mg/kg/h respiration figure, a 20.39% weight-loss figure, a day-21 ethylene peak, and a potato
50–100 g damage threshold.

**Limits on what survived.** In the pomegranate study, storage temperature dominates respiration
and ethylene magnitude (~5× higher CO₂ at 22 °C than 5 °C) and the pairwise dose p-values are not
isolated, so the dose effect is **temperature-confounded**. Banana is **climacteric**, so the
impact effect rides on an autocatalytic ethylene rise and must not be generalised to
non-climacteric produce. Both papers measure ethylene, not a ripening-rate model.

**Verdict:** the mechanism is plausible standard postharvest physiology, unquantified by any study
in the evidence base. It cannot carry an argument.

## What survives as motivation

> **⚠ Provenance warning — read before using this argument.**
>
> What follows **was never submitted to the sweeps**. The terms `micronutrient` and `hidden hunger`
> return zero hits across all three raw research outputs. It is the author's own reasoning from the
> commodity breakdown above, **not a verified finding**, and it did not survive adversarial
> verification because it never entered it.
>
> An earlier draft of this chapter described it as "the only one that survived three sweeps." That
> framing borrowed authority the claim had not earned, and is retracted.

With that caveat: loss concentrates in fruits, vegetables, roots and tubers — the perishable,
high-moisture, mechanically fragile commodities — while cereals, which barely bruise, lose at a
third the rate. Those same perishable categories are the conventional dietary sources of several
micronutrients whose deficiency is widespread.

If that overlap holds, the argument for working on produce damage is about **nutritional quality
rather than tonnage**: the food being lost is disproportionately the food that is not
calorie-substitutable. That is a narrower and more defensible claim than the one this study started
with.

**It is also unverified.** Before it is used to justify effort, it needs the treatment the rest of
this book received: a sweep against nutrition literature establishing whether the commodity overlap
is as tight as it appears, and whether mechanical damage specifically — rather than spoilage or
cold-chain failure — is what removes those foods from the chain. Neither has been done.
